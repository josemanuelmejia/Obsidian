At a global scale, the current peak of mobilization developing since 2017 is the corresponding one to the wave of social struggles initiated at 2008. The mass mobilizations occurred in 100 countries since 2017, and in 30 of them the protests resulted in presidential crisis. This upward scale shift of social struggles have been interpreted as an indicator of the advanced status of the crisis of USA hegemony. As the correspondent one to the over-accumulation crisis of global capitalism developing accelerated since 1970’s. The authors studied the Black Lives Matters (BLM) mobilizations in USA 2020 as a case of a broader set of contentious episodes conceptualized as a global revolt. The development of a revolutionary situation as defined by Lenin, depends on the character of the revolutionary process. The authors found four problems for that development. The first is the fragmentation of massive mobilizations and its disjuncture with the existent challenger organizations (left).  The second is the state-centered condition of capitalist political contention, as an obstacle to transnational revolution and a dynamic prone to cooptation.

The datafication of water infrastructure and its implications for (il)legible water consumers 

Supervisory control and data acquisition (SCADA) systems are increasingly being deployed in cities to monitor and control infrastructures—such as those in charge of providing water. Smart data technologies have been thought to make these processes efficient, secure and equitable. However, smart cities have also raised concerns about surveillance, misrepresentations of people, privacy and data property rights. Hoefsloot et. al. (2022) argue that water datification and smartening respond to practices of legibility, which are layered by multiple sets of data, that shape Lima’s hydrosocial geography differentiably according to categories of water consumers. By following how SEDAPAL make water flows legible in three scales (household, the distribution of water and mapping urban settlements), the authors show how legibility making, rather than creating simple and homogeneous categories, generates complex processes of differentiation that define the relation between water consumers and the state.  Water datafication in Lima shows how the sociopolitical and historical situatedness of data production not only reproduces inequality, but creates forms of surveillance and other complicated (and perhaps dangerous) relations and categories such as the “illegal” consumption of water. Hoefsloot et. al. (2022) suggested that rather than being linear processes of homogenization and standardization, datafication and smartening of water in Lima is contingent and heterogeneous. 

Hoefsloot et. al. (2022) made me think about the nuances of producing contingent and differentiated categories. Sometimes we think that by having multiple categories we can fix representation problems, but if those categories are not politicized and historicized it generates the same reproduction of inequalities as homogenizing data. The second thing I found interesting about this article is the ideas of modernity and the role of the state embedded in smart cities narratives and water datafication. More specifically I am curious about the creation of “illegality” and surveillance in water datafication.%%
