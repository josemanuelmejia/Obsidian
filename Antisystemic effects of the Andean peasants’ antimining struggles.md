# Introduction
The modern world-system structural processes producing antisystemic resistances are vital for the reproduction of the capitalist world economy. The antimining peasants’ struggles in the Andes of South America obstruct effectively the conversion of the money invested as mining productive capital by different imperialist bourgeoisies in competition for the extension of its reproduction circuit scale employing its militarized colonial policy over the territories cursed by the abundance of mineral commodities under different forms of militarism. By military intervention as in Latin America, or as new imperialist wars as initially expressed in Ukraine and Gaza ongoing multilevel polity conflicts. Although past mining contention waves of antisystemic resistance occurred during transitional stages of capitalism modern world-system development -i.e. as the mining proletariat struggles developed in Peru and Great Britain during the first quarter of the 1900’s, or in China during the first years of the second-, the current restructuration attempt of the modern capitalist world-economy not only depend on a completed transition in the afterwards of U.S. hegemony breakdown. Due to the advanced symptoms of the overaccumulation crisis and its attempted resolution, first through the liberalization of the gold price out of the financial institutions of Bretton Woods in 1971 (Fig. 1), and then through the extension of the mining extractive frontier particularly of gold production motivated by the demand of monetary gold holdings for national reserve’s banks exponentially since 2001; the tendency to accumulate monetary gold in national reserves that was strongly monopolistic under U.S. financial capital until the begining of the new millenia began to be followed not only by the European central banks but also by Russia, India, and specifically China. The last mentioned great interstate power increased its acquisition of gold for its national reserves, as its superiority in monetary holdings compared to U.S. (Fig. 2) requires a balance in proportion to its monetary-gold holdings (Fig.3) to overcome the U.S. dollar as hegemonic currency, and intensified its capital production by gold mining in national contexts under which the situation of sinoamerican shared imperialism remains, as in Latin America. The comparison between mining proletariat struggles and anti-mining peasant struggles is expressing more than a idenity shift, it is evidencing that the contradictory tendencies of capital production by the increasing subordination of labor by proletarianization and superexplotation which created the class antisystemic movements of the XX century Figura 1.
