The decade of the 'seventies has been characterised by radical and
often dramatic changes in the sphere of international capitalist
relations. Political changes apart, the period has seen a marked
escalation in the price of oil, raging world inflation, recessions in
all the world's leading industrial economies and an increasing
tendency towards poverty and even bankruptcy in many
countries of the so-called Third World. Running parallel with
these developments has been the phenomenon of a massive rise
in the gold price and, concomitant with this, an unprecedented
resurgence of public interest in the metal.
The complex task of understanding these seemingly disparate
phenomena and drawing out the links between them has been
undertaken by many on the left, but often largely with a view to
examining their implications for a particular country rather than
examining their implications for the international capitalist
system itself. In this article, I intend to try and explain some of
the recent developments in the price of gold by reference to
gold's historical role in the capitalist monetary system and by
trying to understand the relation between the changing form of
that system and the broader crises and pressures that are inherent
in the world capitalist economy. Although this article will inevit-
ably leave many key questions unanswered, I hope that the
systematic presentation of the historical evolution of gold's role
in the monetary system will contribute to the debate about the
nature of the present world economic crisis and its effect on the
monetary system.
The first section of the article will outline the classical
Marxist theory of the various functions which money performs in
the capitalist system. This exposition provides a theoretical basis
for the subsequent historical discussion of the role gold came to play in the monetary system and also of the reasons for ti
changing nature of that system over time. The following section will provide a detailed analysis of the role of gold in the point
World War II phase of capital accumulation, with particular strenght being laid on the conditions which gave rise to the current crisis in capitalism and on the effects of that crisis on gold's role in the monetary system.

Money does not arise by convention, any more than the state does. It arises out of exchange; it is a product of the same.' (Marx, 1973, p. 165)

According to Marx, the capitalist exchange process (the basis in which may be expressed in the syllogism, C- M -C) can only  function systematically if the commodities which are exchanged have as their value a common property, human labour, by which they can be measured against each other. The human labor
 which is embodied in the commodity is measured by social necessary labour time. The value of all commodities is thus determined by the amount of human labour, measured in terms of socially necessary labour time, which they embody and which provides the basis for their exchange. The actual measurement
commodities, as values, occurs through the medium of money. :

Marx's own words:

`The first chief function of money is to supply commodities with the material for the expression of their values, or it represent their values as magnitudes of the same denomination, qualitatively equal, and quantitatively comparable  (Marx, 1974, p. 97)

It is important to stress, though, that although money is the means by which commodities are measured with each other, if not money which provides the basis for the measurement. Money is only the material for the expressionof the values of commodities. It is human labour measured in terms of labour-time which the basis of the equation and comparison  -ie. the measurement of commodities. Money only representsthe value of commodities; it does not give commodities their values.

However, the function of money in the exchange process also determines the form which money takes. In order to fulfil its function as a measure of value, money must itself have value - must itself be a commodity whose value is determined in the same way as is the value of other commodities (by the amount of human labour they embody). Thus, in order to function as money -as general value-equivalent-money must of necessity adopt the commodity-form.

But money fulfils a number of other functions in addition to its main function as a measure of value. The first of these is that it acts as a store of value. The function of money as a store value arises out of, the structure of the exchange process itself. In that process producers and consumers are not directly and
continuously linked. Hence, the two separate processes of buying and selling require to be linked before a system of exchange can
develop-and it is money which provides the necessary link combining the two poles of the syllogism, C - M -C. This
function of money again determines its form, for in order to act as store of value money must appear both as the general universal
form of value, `the common form into which all commodities as exchange values are transformed' and as `a particular commodity alongside the others' (Marx,1973, p. 165). That is, money must again of necessity assume the commodity-form. In this sense, then,
these two functions of money, as a measure of value and as an
store of value, are closely related, since in order to carry out both
these functions money must function as a value-equivalent.
The commodity-form is not, however, essential to the third
function of money. This function relates to the role of money
as the means by which the circulation of commodities in exchange occurs: ie. money appears as the actual means of exchange
(or in Marx's words, as the `medium of circulation') in the
exchange process. In this sense, although money functions
initially in commodity-form, the historical development of the
exchange process renders the commodity-form increasingly irrelevant to the money-function. The development of technology and
extension of the market reduces the spatial and temporal separation of the two poles of the syllogism, C- M -C. Indeed, the
sale and purchase of commodities now occurs with hardly any
time-gap at all and, consequently, money plays no more than a
fleeting role in the process, acting simply as a unit of account to
facilitate the circulation of commodities. This function of money
is thus clearly differentiated from the former functions in that
money no longer has to function as a value-equivalent-it no
longer has to realise the value of a commodity. However, even
more important, this latter function of money now appears as
contradictory to the former functions. For whereas the commodity-form of money is essential to money's functions as a
value-equivalent, that same form is not only irrelevant to, but
actually hampers, its function as a means of exchange (in that the
material bulk of money-commodities like gold, silver, furs or
cows, hampers efficiency). It is this contradiction which opens
the way for token substitutes like paper-money to be introduced
to the exchange process. For these substitutes, which represent
but do not contain the real value of money, facilitate the
function of money as a means of exchange. They do not, however, impinge upon the functions of moneyasa value-equivalent.
Hence, while commodity circulation is necessary to production, the circulation of money in its commodity-form is not
necessary to commodity circulation and, consequently, commodity-money comes to be replaced by other circulating media
which represent commodity-money. This is the objective basis, not only for the historical origin of paper-money, but also for the
state's control and regulation of the money supply in the nations
economy. The functions of money as, on the one hand, a measure
and store of value and, on the other, a means of exchange can be
distinguished both in value terms and in historical terms (in the
actual historical development of the exchange process). First
whereas in fulfilling the former functions money must have ar
intrinsic value of its own, this requirement is not necessary foi
the fulfillment of its latter function; and, second, the former
functions of money are historically prior to the latter function
It was in the period of the earlier markets, when the exchange
of goods was only occasional and spasmodic, that producers required to receive, in return for the sale of their particular commodity, a money-commodity which functioned as a general value
equivalent. It is the actual historical development of the market
and in particular its development under capitalism (with the in
creasing division of labour and expansion of production which
that system entails), that leads to the transformation of the exchange process into a continuous and consistent system in whicl
the functions of money as a measure and store of value become
subordinated to its function as means of exchange. Consequently
as a result of this transformation, the requirement of money to
assume the commodity-form in the actual exchange process is
reduced. The predominance of the rapid circulation of commodities under capitalism means that commodities are constantl
changing hands and that the function of money to actually realise the value of a commodity seemingly disappears. But according to Marx, despite the disappearance of money's function as a value-equivalent at the level ofappearancesunder capitalism in essence money never loses this function.

This contradiction between what occurs at the level of
appearances and reality under capitalism emerges clearly in term
of economic crises. For example, in a crisis of over-production
commodities stream onto the market but cannot be absorbed and
consequently the even rhythm of circulation (the smooth flow o
commodities) is disrupted and exchange breaks down. Now
money can no longer function as a means of exchange since
exchange itself has collapsed. At this point the other function of
money (as general value-equivalent) reasserts itself with
vengeance. Now everyone wants money, but not paper-money
which has no real value of its own. They want commodity
money; money in which value itself is stored; money in the form
of the general value-equivalent of the commodity, which can be
held indefinitely or at least until the crisis is over and then exchanged for any particular commodity (for any particular exchange value).[ 1]
This function of money, as means of payment, is not
identical with money's functions as measure and store of value
However, since it involves the employment of money as a general
value-equivalent, it can be grouped together with those other functions of money (as measure and store of value) in which
money functions as a general value equivalent. As such, a similar
condition is attached to its form in fulfilling its function as means
of payment: ie. it assumes the commodity-form of the general
value-equivalent. This means that other `profane' forms of
money, e.g. paper-money, should not be substituted for it.
However,asa result of the expansion of financial and banking
structures under advanced capitalism such substitution of commodity-money as means of payment does in fact occur over the
short-term. For instance, the development of the credit system means that paper-money does temporarily come to represent commodity-money in its means of payment function.[2] But it can neverentirely replace commodity-money in this function because ultimately, and particularly in times of crisis, real pay-
ment-i.e. payment in terms of the generally exchangeable value-
equivalent-is demanded.[3]
However, the fact that some replacement does occur opens
the way to confusion. For the paper-money which circulates in
modern capitalist society subsumes under itself these two money
functions: the means of exchange function, in which form it is
`true' paper-money, and the means of payment function, in which
form it is 'profane'.[4] In its `true' function as means of
exchange, paper-money must simply facilitate the circulation of
commodities: it is never called upon to realise the value of com-
modities and therefore the problem of its relation to the stock of
the money-commodity (gold in modern capitalist society)-the
problem of convertibility-does not arise in this case. This is not
the case, however, in the case of its `profane' function as means
of payment where it is issued in lieu of real payment (for
instance, as credit): in this case there must necessarily be a strict
correlation between the value the paper-money carries and the
actual amount of money-commodity (gold) it represents-that
is the issue of convertibility is paramount-because although the credit systemdeferspayment, ultimately payment still has to be made.
A further confusion in the relationship between the money-
commodity, gold and paper-money arises as a result of the fact that gold no longer plays any obvious role in the domestic capitalist economies. This follows the abolition by the leading
capitalist powers of domestic convertibility in the early decades
of the 20th century. It was the extension of capitalist relations
to all sectors of the domestic economies, accompanied by an
increasing concentration of capital, which made it possible for
these states to bring the domestic exchange systems under their
control. The state literally took over the function of gold as
means of payment by acting as guarantor of the credit system. As
a result the money-commodity gold, came to be formally
abolished from the domestic economies.[5] However, as we
noted above, when the state itself is threatened the issue of convertibility-of the money commodity-immediately leaps to therefore again.

But what was possible on a domestic level has not proved to be as easily possible at the international level. For the basis of the state's regulation of domestic exchange lay in the fact that under advanced capitalism a relatively high level of concentration a production was achieved. Attempts to increase the concentratio of production on a world scale have inevitably produced various economic and political crises as competing national capital challenged each other for hegemony. Despite these set-back;however, concentration of production on a world scale has pre ceeded and is in fact accompanied by a more sophisticated extension of exchange machinery beyond the immediate confines of the nation state. The EEC is an example of this kind of political extension on a regional basis while the World Bank and the IMF provide examples of different kinds of political support structure which create further conditions for the concentration of production on a world scale. But it is necessary to stress that it is not possible to move in a linear fashion from an argument regarding state control over the exchange system within a domestic economy to an argument regarding political control over the international capitalist exchange system itself, since the latter qualitatively different from the former. To achieve a unitary world capitalist state with legislative powers which bind all competing capitalist nations would require, in the first instance, a united and powerful world ruling class which has no base in a particular nation state. (I shall explore later what happens when
a particular capitalist ruling class located in a particular national state attempts to become a world ruling class.)

Whether such an unlikely eventuality is possible or not is not the object of this investigation. What I shall attempt to do, however, is to examine the way in which the historical development of the exchange system under capitalism has led to pressures of this kind-pressures to squeeze commodity-money out of the international exchange system altogether and to replace it by state-dominated international exchange system. I shall then examine the way in which a particular national capitalist power as the United States of America, sought to project its currency in worldwide circulation as a substitute for gold in an attempt to gain control over the international exchange system (and there by, over other national capitals). And finally, I shall exploit the effects of this on the international system of exchange and specifically, on gold's role within it.
