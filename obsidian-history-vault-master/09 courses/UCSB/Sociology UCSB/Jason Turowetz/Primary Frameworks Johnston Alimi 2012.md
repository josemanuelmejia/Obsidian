## Abstract

Expanding on McAdam, Tarrow and Tilly's approach to the study of contentious episodes, we develop a conceptual framework to analyze front-stage politics in the light of back-stage cultural and interpretative processes, drawing upon the Chechen and the Palestinian national movements. We elaborate on Goffman's original notion of ‘primary frameworks’ to capture the influence of fundamental cultural templates, and his concept of ‘keying processes’ to capture the way primary frameworks are reworked in the dynamics of political contention. We then identify three central components of primary frameworks, namely, collective identity (the _subject_), what the subject does (the _verb_) and who or what is the object of those actions (the _object_). This article identifies the primary frameworks and keying processes of Chechens and Palestinians with relation to Russia and Israel, respectively. It then traces how they are played out in the heat of political contention as changes in the structure of political opportunities and threats unfold, and how they combine to drive a process of Islamization. We conclude by discussing how the subject—verb—object triplet of primary frameworks helps specify the dynamic interpretative work in political contention.

In recent years, the study of social movements has been challenged – and in part, refocused – by Doug McAdam, Sidney Tarrow and Charles Tilly's _Dynamics of Contention_ ([McAdam _et al._, 2001](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr35-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)). Their project attempts to shift structural and political process analyses towards a more dynamic, relational analysis of ‘episodes of contention’, that is, claim making and challenges to the state occurring over the long term. To this end, they have sought to identify causal mechanisms that are generalizable across the nominally distinct sub-fields of nationalism, social movements, revolutions and democratic transitions. While McAdam _et al._'s previous research was based strongly on structuralist analysis, important elements of the _Dynamics of Contention_ program focused on cultural and interpretative processes ([McAdam and Tarrow, 2011](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr34-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), pp. 6–7). The authors sought to move beyond the framing perspective – at that time, the dominant cultural focus in the political protest literature – to analyze the ‘interactive construction of disputes among challengers, opponents, the state, and the media’ ([McAdam _et al._, 2001](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr35-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 44). This is a view that focuses on three key dimensions: first, the ongoing forging of new collective identities (‘identity shift’ and ‘actor constitution’ in the authors' terminology of mechanisms); second, new definitions of action (‘polarization’ and ‘scale shift’); and third, definitions and redefinitions of opportunity and threat (the mechanism of ‘opportunity—threat spirals’). Simplified, cultural elements of the _Dynamics of Contention_ can be thought of as anchored in the ongoing definition and redefinition of (1) the subject, (2) its actions and (3) the target or object of its actions.

We apply this tripartite view of cultural interpretative work to analyze the interplay among enduring patterns of culture and dynamic political processes, using the Islamization of Chechen and Palestinian nationalism. We focus attention on the definition and redefinition of collective identities, action repertoires and targets against the backdrop of ‘primary frameworks’, to use Erving Goffman's original term ([Goffman, 1974](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr17-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), pp. 21–6). These are the basic cognitive schemata – consistent with what Roberto [Franzosi (1999](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr13-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x); [2004](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr14-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)) calls the <subject> <verb> <object> semantic triplet – that orient activities and come to be widely shared through social experience. We argue that knowing a group's primary frameworks, just as it helps understanding of group behavior, also helps in understanding the ongoing interpretative processes of political contention, but it is only a starting point. As social experience progresses, Goffman reminds us, new layers, or _frame rims_ ([Goffman, 1974](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr17-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 82) are extended on primary frameworks through a process he labeled _keying_– a process that captures both the primary framework basis of framing and, in political contention, how they are transformed into something new. Regarding the analysis of collective action frames, our point is that, much like certain molecules, which cannot combine with others to form new compounds unless there are appropriate receptors, one cannot impose just any social construction (frame rim) upon a primary framework. Such a notion is implicitly captured by the concept of cultural resonance as an important aspect of successful collective action frames ([Snow and Benford, 1988](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr47-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x); [Snow _et al._, 1986](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr48-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)) and by [Marc Ross' (2007)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr42-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x) concept of cultural expressions, but without sufficient recognition of the layering process and what lies at its base. It is fair to say that research on the framing of political protest tends to de-emphasize the notion of primary frameworks as a widely shared and fundamental cultural stock, but rather tends to focus on strategic framing by leaders and organizations in the heat of contention ([Benford, 1993](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr2-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x); [Ellingson, 1995](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr11-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x); [Ferree _et al._, 2002](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr12-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x); [Ross, 2007](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr42-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)).

In line with McAdam _et al._, we recognize that primary frameworks can be seen both as a resource to be strategically used and as a constraint on the breadth of strategic decisions, depending on the unfolding of the contentious episode. It is fair to say, however, that when _Dynamics of Contention_ draws upon cultural factors, it presents a strong agency-based, performance-oriented analysis which leaves the details of the culture—agency nexus fairly underdeveloped. McAdam _et al._ recognize that their treatise is but a first step, and leave the door open for theoretical reformulation, specification and empirical exploration. In this vein, we explore in the pages that follow how strong cultural influences may shape the unfolding of political protest.

We develop a conceptual framework to the analysis of what may be called ‘front-stage’ politics in light of ‘back-stage’ cultural and interpretative processes, as a way to understand how primary cultural patterns may be relevant in protest trajectories. We suggest that in some movements: (1) primary cultural frameworks may be more relevant than in others; (2) this may be at different junctures in their development; and (3) when relevant, they may strongly shape how subsequent frame rims are layered upon them in ongoing political contention – not only with opposing groups but also among ostensible allies within the same broad movement.

This article provides rich historical evidence from the national struggle of the Palestinian (1987–2000) and Chechen (1987–96) movements. We will show how cultural identity, long-standing ethnic conflicts and religious influences comprise deep cultural patterns which get ‘keyed’ and extended in the fray of political contention. It immediately comes to mind that such deep cultural influences are relevant to other nationalist mobilizations ([Beissinger, 2002](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr1-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x); [Brubaker and Laitin, 1998](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr5-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x); [Johnston, 1994](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr25-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x); [Ross, 2007](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr42-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)) but, also, we see similar processes in movements where activists combine the raw materials of different cultural texts, oppositional and hegemonic, in creative ways ([Steinberg, 1999](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr49-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x); [Wright, 2007](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr54-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)). We proceed by analyzing the two episodes of contention according to the primary cultural elements in each case, focusing on how they are played out across varying political contexts and in different frameworks of interaction.

## Primary Frameworks and Text Structure

Although fundamental to Goffman's analysis of ordinary interpretative processes, the term ‘primary framework’ has not found its way into social movement analysis, which has generally been content with the general term ‘collective action frame’ as a basis for elaboration of processes relevant to protest mobilization ([Johnston and Noakes, 2005](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr26-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)). [Goffman (1974](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr17-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 27) proposed that social actors draw upon numerous primary frameworks to make sense of daily life, which together constitute part of shared culture. He observes that social actors are probably ‘unaware of such organized features as the \[primary\] framework has and unable to describe the framework with any completeness if asked, yet these handicaps are not a bar to \[them\] easily and fully applying it’ ([Goffman, 1974](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr17-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 31). Goffman begins his _Frame Analysis_ (1974) with an elaboration of the functions and workings of primary frameworks in a way that parallels how most scholars would commonly understand the workings of collective action frames. Later in his analysis he traces how primary frameworks are transformed through interaction and experience, building upon them in a way that keeps the _primary_ structure at the core. Primary frameworks are fundamental interpretative constructs.

By themselves, primary frameworks may suggest a strong cultural determinism, but Goffman holds that they are merely the groundwork of ongoing interpretation, extended and modified by what he calls the ‘keying processes’. The idea is that primary frameworks can be changed, or ‘played in a different key’ ([Goffman, 1974](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr17-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 44), resulting in the interpretation of what is going on being altered, sometimes only slightly, but sometimes drastically. The basic structure stays, but elaborations on the ‘rims’ of primary frameworks change the meaningfulness that is conveyed, as they are the most recent and most accessible elements of interpretation.

There are two basic questions that we pose. First, given that primary frameworks are operative at numerous levels of social life, can we identify the ones that are invoked in cases of contentious collective action? Second, although Goffman does not specify their structure, can we approximate a basic schematic to permit a systematic way of analyzing whether primary frameworks have a role in political contention?

A useful starting point for approximating primary framework structures is the aforementioned basic tripartite relation of subject—action—target. Together they can identify the basic ‘story grammars’ of a conflict, a concept proposed by Franzosi as a way to analyze protest events ([Franzosi, 1999](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr13-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x); [2004](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr14-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)). His idea is based on the fact that all action follows a simple grammatical structure comprised of <subject> <verb> <object>, and that additional modifiers then give shape and substance to descriptions of collective action. The modifiers would be type, number, structure or character for the <subject> and the <object>, and time, space or type for the <action>. Franzosi calls this structure a ‘semantic triplet’. He proposes its use as an analytical method to capture the essential ingredients of collective action: the collective ‘we’ of the acting subject, the action of contention and the object of the contentious act.

We propose that the basic semantic triplet structure captures relevant relations of framing processes in contentious episodes, ‘where subjects are related to their actions, actors and actions to their modifiers, and subjects and objects (both social actors) related to one another via their actions’ ([Franzosi, 1999](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr13-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 133). Applied historically and comparatively, Franzosi's semantic triplets present one way that a movement's challenge can be thought of textually by specifying a fundamental subject—verb—object triplet which comprises a primary framework and by tracing its permutations in contentious episodes when the political context shifts. This is the strategy we will follow in this article.

We assume that there is a wide variety of primary frameworks in Chechen and Palestinian societies. Inasmuch as we are interested in political challenges, the fundamental triplet will take the generic form of <collective we> <challenges, makes a claim, resists or mobilizes against> <target or opponent>. Regarding our two cases, the skeletal structures take the form <Chechens> <make claims against> <Russians>, and <Palestinians> <make claims against> <Israelis>. The task at hand is, first, to reconstruct this skeletal structure based on the collective history, shared symbols and cultural images relevant to each case (see [Figure 1](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#fig1-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)). Second, we examine how some elaborations of this structure are permitted and others limited such that battles over what it means to be a Chechen, or what the best action strategy should be, will guide the movement's trajectory. This is the keying process whereby different groups within the movement seek to add new frame rims that reflect their own interests, but emphasize distinct elaborations of the basic cultural stock. Different rims will appear to predominate at different points in the movement's trajectory. As opportunities and threats unfold, the process of keying primary frameworks produces a complex episode of collective action.

In both cases, despite the availability of more secular and pragmatic cultural elements, contention eventually took the form of fundamentalist Islamic Palestinian/Chechen people waging Islamic-based holy war against Israelis/Russians. Yet we argue that this was not simply a reflection of a group's ideology (see [Levitt, 2006](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr30-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x); [Litvak 1998](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr33-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x) for the Palestinian case, and [Henze 1995](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr20-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x); [Tishkov 2004](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr51-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x) for the Chechen case). Rather it was a product of political and organizational contexts that gradually privileged the Islamic-based elements of the primary framework. The analysis that follows traces the historical emergence of the <subject> for both Palestinian and Chechen nationalism. We then do this also for the <predicate>, combining the analysis of the <verb> and the <object> because they are in a close and dynamic relationship with each other (see [Figure 1](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#fig1-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)).

## The Subject: The Dynamics of ‘Who We Are’

At the core of Palestinian collective identity is a connectedness to the land, which came to be represented in the Palestinian motherland. Palestinians are an Arab people, of whom most are Sunni Muslims and (prior to the 1967 Israeli military occupation) cultivators of land and/or shepherds. They have lived on this land for centuries, and their tie to it has been strongly shaped by continuing occupation and reoccupation by foreign forces. This occupation underlies the current struggle with the state of Israel, and historically has generated three self-images, all rooted in the Islamic tradition. The first and most central self-image is the _Sahmed_, the Palestinian peasant, the survivor who steadfastly clings to his land, notwithstanding the pressures of occupation and expropriation ([Shehadeh, 1982](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr44-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)). Second is the _Fida'y_, the holy warrior who throws himself into battle, willing to sacrifice his life in the struggle. Third, the _Shahid_ (witness) is a young man who fights against all odds and offers the collective good, a reflection of his unwavering Islamic faith ([Kimmerling and Migdal, 1999](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr29-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)). These two self-images evolved after the first.

With the surge of Zionist migration to Palestine, the 1948 national catastrophe (i.e. _al-Nakba_) and, in 1967, the occupation of the territories by Israel,<sup><a href="https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#fn1-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x" role="doc-noteref" id="body-ref-fn1-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x">1</a></sup> a gradual keying of these three primary images began, as part of a new rim for the subject's definition, namely Palestinian nationalism. Occupation gradually made the nationalist rim of identity much more central compared with the other Islamic and Arab ones. True, long before the Zionist migration to Palestine there existed a distinct and widely shared Palestinian collective memory with several overlapping self-definitions ([Doumani, 1992](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr9-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x); [Khalidi, 1988](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr28-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)). Palestinians perceived themselves, for example, as part of the Ottoman world, the Arab world and the Islamic world. However, Palestinians inside the occupied territories were first and foremost an occupied people. They were, according to Ibrahim [Daqqaq (1983)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr6-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), a nationalist public figure in the territories, the people _on_ the land, who experienced in body and mind an ongoing hardship. Thus, throughout the 1980s and into the first Intifada (1987–92), a Palestinian nationalism closely linked to the land enjoyed widespread popularity. This territories-based collective identity was promoted by the local Palestine Liberation Organization (PLO)-led nationalist camp which gradually assumed a central role in decision making and strategizing, at times even against the interests and directives of the Tunis-based PLO (see also [Hiltermann, 1991](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr21-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 49).

On the eve of the Intifada, the popular PLO-led nationalist camp was central to the articulation of a definition of ‘who we are’ by drawing upon these elements but steering clear of ideological rigidity in order to embrace various factions within one organizational framework. Nationalism was developed into an umbrella ideology which consisted of various elements of identity, including religious ones. It provided a first keying of the primary framework within which any Palestinian, regardless of his personal ideological commitment (e.g. Marxism or Islam), or personal socio-economic situation, could identify. By not rejecting Islam, establishing an infrastructure of collaboration with Islamic groups,<sup><a href="https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#fn2-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x" role="doc-noteref" id="body-ref-fn2-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x">2</a></sup> acknowledging the merits of leftist ideologies, glorifying the role and status of the _fellahin_ (peasants) and promoting the distinctly traditional Palestinian identity, the PLO's nationalism linked with the primary frameworks of Palestinian identity ([Swedenburg, 1990](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr50-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)).

The idea of Palestinian self-determination meant a promise of belonging within their own national territory, ownership of property, a measure of control over their lives and the ability to participate and design their own destiny – all of which rested on an authentic interpretation of the Palestinian primary framework. But another contender to define Palestinian identity was the Islamic Jihad in Palestine (IJP), which promoted an identity based on Islam as a faith of liberation ([Hatina, 2001](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr19-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)). Yet at this time the Islamic Jihad's rim was problematic on two grounds: (1) it called for a total, uncompromising transformation of the self; and (2) a mode of action (i.e. terrorist activity) that entailed great sacrifice and risk. While both aspects of identity built upon the _Fida'y_ and _Shahid_ elements of the primary framework, they required a degree of commitment that had limited appeal.

Yet another contender was the Muslim Brotherhoods (MB), which offered a transnational identity, the nation of Islam, which could have been attractive had the Palestinians not been occupied and displaced from their land. Even after the formation of Hamas as the political arm of the MB in late December 1987, there was no immediate attempt to embrace a distinct Palestinian identity. The result was that MB/Hamas refrained from openly opposing the nationalist forces, and tended to accept a _modus vivendi_ with them, namely, a tacit understanding to avoid confrontation and lessen ideological disagreements.

This truce began to disintegrate as a result of King Hussein's declaration of Jordan's disengagement from the West Bank and recognition of the PLO as the sole, legitimate representative of the Palestinian people, coupled with the decision at the Arab Summit of June 1988 in Algeria to allocate funds for the PLO. The recognition of the nationalists as the sole and legitimate representative of the Palestinian people immediately led to the publication of the Hamas Charter in August 1988.

The Charter was essentially a public declaration of the MB/Hamas as a political power competing with the PLO over the issue of who represents the Palestinian people. It was the first time the group declared itself as a distinct Palestinian-Islamic political force. As it reads:

> The Islamic Resistance Movement found itself at a time when Islam has disappeared from life. … Nationalism of the Islamic Resistance Movement is part of its religion … Its members have been fed on that. Secularism completely contradicts religious ideology. … with all our appreciation for The Palestinian Liberation Organization – and what it can develop into – and without belittling its role in the Arab—Israeli conflict, we are unable to exchange the present or future Islamic Palestine with the secular idea (available from the Avalon Project's website, [http://avalon.law.yale.edu/20th\_century/hamas.asp](http://avalon.law.yale.edu/20th_century/hamas.asp)).

In the next year, Islamic elements of ‘who we are’ promoted by MB/Hamas intensified, primarily due to the decline in the political and public status of Yasser Arafat throughout late 1989 and 1990. Arafat's status was seriously damaged domestically and internationally during the build-up of the Gulf crisis. First, this derived partly from a series of proposals for a political solution to the Palestinian question proposed by Egypt, the US and Israel between mid-1989 and early 1990, all of which included no recognition of the PLO. Second, PLO—US dialogue was suspended in June 1990, triggered by PLO terrorism against Israel on 30 May. Third, Arafat's unconditional support for Saddam Hussein's invasion of a Sunni Muslim state in August 1990 played a role. Together, these factors decreased significantly the flow of Gulf state money to the nationalists, caused domestic and international criticism and led to a heavier Israeli crackdown. This is a pattern that also occurred in Chechnya, when similar exogamous factors privileged a jihadist frame rim over a less radical nationalist one.

All this contributed to the growing popularity of Hamas among Palestinians and its keying of an Islam-based <subject> ([Mishal and Sela, 1999](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr37-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)). During this time, the religious ardor of the struggle gradually increased, observable on both sides of the conflict. The PLO's waning popularity led to the September 1990 agreement with Hamas which was aimed at lessening the tension so as to avert deterioration into an all-out internal war. Yet the agreement did not end the struggle for legitimacy and power. What practically rebalanced the situation for nationalists was the intense Israeli crackdown on MB/Hamas as a result of Hamas' terrorist actions. Israel, fully aware of the potential danger from Hamas, engaged in a systematic oppression of the MB organizational network. Israel's familiarity with the Hamas leaders, the result of traditional fostering of the MB's activities and operations throughout the early 1980s in the hope that they would counterbalance the nationalist camp, facilitated the crackdown. The arrest of most activists and of the MB's spiritual leader, Ahmed Yassin, in mid-1989 brought about a significant decrease in the movement's power and influence.

Things became more intense and lethal with the commencement of Palestinian self-rule in 1992, the Oslo Accords and the subsequent formation of the Palestinian Authority in 1994. Already during the self-rule elections in the territories in 1992 numerous deadly confrontations broke out between the parties. The challenge posed by Hamas to the Fatah-led Palestinian Authority was translated into systematic attempts at undermining the Oslo Accords through the use of numerous suicide bombings against Israel between early 1994 and 1996. As Arafat's difficulties in delivering the promise of Oslo increased (difficulties for which Hamas was partly responsible) Hamas' power increased significantly.

Additionally, the Palestinian Authority became the target of widespread criticism, mounted not only by the Islamists but also by considerable segments of the Palestinian population and the grass-roots forces that actually made the Intifada. These forces got pushed aside by the old-guard PLO members, who were contemptuously referred to as the ‘Tunisians’, those who were outsiders and did not represent the Palestinian people. In response, Arafat made systematic attempts to appease the Islamic forces, for example, making references to religious symbols in his public speeches, frequently portraying himself as the leader of an army of _Shahids_ marching to liberate the holy city as did Salah al-Din,<sup><a href="https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#fn3-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x" role="doc-noteref" id="body-ref-fn3-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x">3</a></sup> and occasionally consulting with Islamic leaders. Yet this did little to contain (or perhaps contributed to) an Islamic-based rim which seemed to have been firmly layered upon the primary framework of Palestinian identity. During 2000 a new, this time Islamic-driven, lethal cycle of contention was consolidated, guided by both nationalist and Islamist groups, with Arafat having little control over the situation.

### The Dynamics of the Chechen Subject

Like the Palestinian case, there is a multifaceted quality to the primary framework of Chechen identity. Chechens shared a self-understanding of freedom and independence ([Gammer, 2006](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr16-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)), especially in the face of the Russian colossus to the north, which was distinct from Chechen society in culture, social structure and religion, and had threatened the region for centuries. Chechen society was historically a ‘warrior peasant democracy based on ownership and collective defense of the land’ ([Derluguian, 1995](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr7-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 126), which, as with the Palestinians, was reflected in a strong attachment to family land and villages, despite Soviet efforts at collectivization and relocation. Permeating Chechen society were large patrilineal extended families that were parts of larger clans (_teip_ in Chechen). These traditional kinship relations persisted into the Soviet period, and were supported by a code of honor, obligation to one's kin and rejection of foreign authority ([Wixman, 1980](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr53-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)).

Along with these patterns, Chechen identity was historically associated with Sufi Islam. In the eighteenth century, Sheikh Mansur helped spread Sufi practice in the region by calling for replacement of pagan practices with sharia (Islamic law), and declaring jihad against infidel Russians. Sufism is organized into two brotherhoods or _tariqats_ in Chechnya. The Naqshbandi _tariqat_ is smaller and located mostly in the northern and eastern lowlands. The Qadiryia _tariqat_ is larger, and perhaps best known for its dramatic _zikrs_, weekly gatherings of 10–40 adepts to perform religious hymns and short prayers, sometimes accompanied by controlled breathing, repetition, song and ecstatic dancing.

The diffuse structure of Sufism and its association with numerous historical revolts against the Russians helps explain its persistence in national identity. More than a decade prior to the nationalist movement (during the height of the Brezhnev years), a Soviet researcher estimated that half of all Muslim believers in the Chechen-Ingush ASSR were members of Sufi _tariqats_. Bennigsen and Wimbush list several characteristics that help explain this persistence ([Bennigsen and Wimbush, 1985a](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr3-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 188): a symbiosis of Sufi organization and clan structure; the clandestine and militantly anti-Russian organization of Sufi orders; and their extensive networks, each _tariqat_ having its own criminal courts and treasury. Sufi brotherhoods also had resources at their disposal that were outside the Soviet state and party control, for example clandestine houses of prayer, Quranic schools and holy shrines of pilgrimage.

Like primary frameworks of the Palestinian ‘We’, the unfolding of Chechen political contention in the context of the demise of the Soviet empire built upon basic cultural elements but was not determined by them. Several distinct frame rims emerged among competing groups in the early phases of the movement. In the late 1980s, Chechen nationalism mostly comprised the urban intelligentsia in Groznyi who focused on issues common to other Soviet ethnonational regions during perestroika and glasnost: democratic reforms, reviving cultural traditions, bringing to light the history of Soviet repression, and ‘eco-nationalism’. Early-riser groups such as the Kavkaz Circle (an intellectual group), the Popular Front of Chechnya and Ingushetia, Union for the Assistance of Perestroika and the Green party were secular, pursued a moderate intellectual nationalism and had many members from the reformist wing of the Communist party ([Derluguian, 2005](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr8-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), pp. 178–85; [Lieven, 1998](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr31-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 57). Their political and economic interests resided in an autonomous Chechnya with greater freedoms, but not independence from the Soviet Union.

Among the nationalists were also younger, more radical groups that criticized the conservative party establishment. For example, a group called Bart party (or Harmony), eventually renamed the Vainakh Democratic party (a more nationalistic name referring to the Chechen-Ingush people) pursued a more extreme nationalism. Unlike the reformists, this camp keyed their demands upon independence (rather than ‘sovereignty’ within the Soviet Union) but, here too, Islamic elements of the primary framework remained muted. At best, the radicals weakly demanded ‘the separation of atheism from the state, i.e. the cessation of anti-Muslim religious persecution, and … the re-establishment of traditional national institutions in the republic such as the Mekjk Kkhel or Council of Elders’ ([Dunlop, 1998](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr10-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 90). This latter demand drew upon traditional clan structure which, as we mentioned, often overlapped with Sufi groups. Later, these radicals called for the replacement of the region's Supreme Soviet by a freely elected Chechen National Congress (CNC), based partly on traditional village relations, as the legislative body of an independent republic separated from Russia and the Soviet Union. The first CNC met on 23–6 November 1990, and became the focus of a struggle between unionist reformers and radical nationalists.

This conflict remained predominately secular, with only occasional cries of ‘Allah Akhbar!’ in the political rallies among competing groups ([Hughes, 2007](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr22-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 24), but the beginnings of a shift appeared in August 1991 in response to the attempted coup of communist hardliners against Mikhail Gorbachev. When news of the putsch became known, the National Congress called upon all Chechens to mobilize. Thousands poured into Groznyi from rural regions to defend the republic and depose the communists from power. On 22 August a huge demonstration took place at Lenin Square, and in the face of the threat, deep cultural patterns were resurrected and infused into the predominately secular nationalist camp. The central plaza was renamed Sheikh Mansur Square, significantly, after the leader of Chechnya's first jihad against the Russians in the late eighteenth century. A huge portrait of him was displayed there, further confirming the mobilizing and unifying power of religious iconography in times of threat. During the coup attempt, references to the Islamic basis of Chechen identity were often voiced – a powerful vocabulary available to nationalist leaders. The names of the historical leaders of anti-Russian jihads were invoked. References to traditional customs and identities, Sufi saints and imams, village organizations (such as councils of elders and Sufi teachers) and clan ties were commonly made. The mayor of Groznyi, Beslan Gantemirov, organized and armed militiamen to defend the city ([Henze, 1995](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr20-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 30). Although Gantemirov was little more than a mafioso ([Hughes, 2007](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr22-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x); [Wilhelmsen, 2006](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr52-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)), it is worth noting that he bestowed upon his cronies and thugs the label ‘Islamic Path party’. Already, invoking Islam, if in name only, could confer a degree of legitimacy and rally the troops in his militia.

Several other militias were formed to mobilize for battle, creating a politically volatile situation in which the means of violence in the republic was dispersed. The battle never came, the coup being defeated in a matter of days, but the disintegration of the Soviet Union then began in earnest. To consolidate his own power in the Russian Republic, Boris Yeltsin urged local leaders to assert their authority against the Kremlin ([Bohlen, 1991](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr4-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)). In one speech, he encouraged ethnic minorities to ‘take as much freedom as you can swallow’, a statement intended as an attack on Soviet central authority but interpreted by Chechen nationalists as opening the door to independence.

This opening prepared the way for new strategies of contention in the region, but the alternative courses of ‘what to do’, the <verb>, were strongly predicated upon the emerging definitions of ‘who we are’, the <subject>, which had a growing Islamic component. Thus, strategic decisions about the course of action in both our cases were not only based on perceptions of opportunity and threat, but also on the emerging rims of the <subject> and, as we will see, on competing definitions of the predicate, the <verb—object> relationship.

## The Predicate: The Dynamics of What to Do (and to Whom)

As with the <subject>, how the <verb> is defined and how the battle is waged depend greatly on political context, generating tension and discord over tactics and strategy. But an additional factor in analyzing the complete predicate is that the <object> component is not passive but rather an active player, whose actions affect how the challenger sees it and what its strategies are towards it. Thus, a movement's action repertoire becomes doubly contingent, first on its keying of the <subject> and, second, on its keying of the <object> – which, in part, is shaped by the object's own actions.

After the defeat of the hardliner putsch, Chechens prepared for autonomous elections to be held on 27 October 1991. One of the first acts of the newly elected president, General Djokar Dudayev, was to claim the republic's independence. Fearing the disintegration of Russia, Boris Yeltsin declared a state of emergency in Chechnya. He sent a contingent of Interior Ministry troops (about 600) to a Chechen airbase to seize weapons and enforce a curfew. Hundreds of thousands of Chechens poured into Groznyi to defend the nation. Among them again were rural Chechens closely linked with Sufi Islamic practice. Their presence among the secular nationalists of Groznyi reinforced an emerging Islamic rim on the definition of the <subject>, drawing on the deep history of religious wars against the Russians and further strengthening the jihadist rim as a means to interpret the invasion of Chechen territory. At rallies, calls for jihad were voiced, cries of ‘Allah Akhbar!’ heard more frequently and, among the green Islamic flags in the crowds ([Bohlen, 1991](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr4-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 6), the linking of past holy wars against the Russians gave definition to the Chechen resistance against Russian invaders. The Russian troops were surrounded at the airport by Chechen militias and their return to Russia negotiated. Significantly, when news of the victory spread to the center of Groznyi, there were spontaneous Sufi _zikr_s in celebration.

The ill-planned invasion invoked elements of the primary framework for all Chechens, most notably, historical resistance to Russia and especially Stalin's mass deportation of Chechens in 1944. Responding to false charges of Chechen collaboration with the Nazis, Stalin ordered the round-up of all Chechens and Ingush (about 500,000) to relocate forcibly the entire population in Central Asia ([Gammer, 2006](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr16-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 166). Several thousand who avoided arrest were hunted down and shot by NKVD troops. According to John [Dunlop (1998](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr10-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 77) about 145,000 of the deportees died in the first four years. All Chechens lost family members, which kept these events fresh in collective memory. The Russian invasion evoked these deep memories of the <object>. Among co-religionists and political leaders, deeper historical references of Islamic-based jihad against Russian colonizers were further animated by the labels ‘criminals’ and ‘bandits’ applied by Yeltsin and Rutskoi to demonize the Chechens. James [Hughes (2007](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr22-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 28) observes that such references invoked the deep narratives of Russian colonization, resistance, deportation and racism, all of which activated historical links with jihad.

Although Dudayev was a secular politician who had been a party member, a major general in the Soviet air force and not an observant Muslim, he increasingly employed Islamic references in his public speeches and Islamic symbolism in political rituals. When he declared Chechen independence, Sufis danced _zikrs_ in the central square ([Gall and de Waal, 1998](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr15-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 32). Although he originally advocated a vague secular vision of nation building ([Hughes, 2007](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr22-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 24), three interrelated factors accentuated the Islamic rim and the accompanying jihadist interpretation of the <verb>. First, Dudayev had to establish his own Islamic credentials. Second, his election and de facto independence from Russia opened up the stage to challenges from competing political forces, often aligned with rival clans and with militias that did not demobilize. Competing politicians and warlords often laid claims to the Islamic mantle of jihadist leaders from the past – an ironic turn because, in most cases, their conversions were recent, suggesting strategic moves to increase support. For Dudayev, a need to bolster his rural base impelled such moves as well – as with his rivals, his Islamic symbolism in political rituals was often a simple case of political spin. Third, the continued threat of Russian aggression invoked elements of historical jihad embedded in the primary framework, as mentioned above, available to all Chechens. During threats of war, vague religious sentiments congeal into a powerful unifying force.

Finally, when Russia invaded with a greater force in winter 1994–5, and for the next eighteen months, calls for jihad became more common. Chechen forces were made up of the National Guard plus several militias led by warlords who varied in their allegiance to Dudayev. Among the most powerful were Shamil Basaev, known for his incursions into Russian territory and who became the best-known Chechen jihadist; Salman Raduev, who organized several anti-Russian terrorist actions and developed strong jihadist ties; Movsar Baraev, who later on led the Moscow theater hostage-taking in 2002; Movladi Ugudov, one of the earliest converts to radical Islam; and Zelimkhan Yandarbiev – an early secular-intellectual leader and vice-president under Dudayev who later became an Islamist (for a fuller account see [Wilhelmsen, 2006](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr52-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x); also [Hughes, 2007](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr22-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 19; [Smith, 2001](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr45-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)).

Jihadist keying solidified the basis of waging war by transcending the factional politics of Chechen independence. Additionally, the brutality of the Russian campaign, which included the bombing of Groznyi and towns and villages suspected as rebel strongholds, and the terrorizing of households with young males, strengthened the Islamic-jihadist rim. Martyrdom and beliefs of rewards in paradise justified the sacrifices of young fighters and imparted fierceness in battle.<sup><a href="https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#fn4-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x" role="doc-noteref" id="body-ref-fn4-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x">4</a></sup> For more than a year, Chechens fought against great odds, inflicting heavy losses among Russian soldiers. Facing elections, Yeltsin pressed for peace. The Khasavyurt agreement was signed on 31 August 1996, ending the conflict and putting off the question of Chechen independence for five years.

### Contention over the Palestinian Verb

During the run-up to the 1987 Intifada, the nationalist forces set the pace and tone of the struggle and managed considerable levels of coordination and unity in terms of action strategy. This was expressed in a systematic collective effort to discipline and coordinate activities as part of the ‘restricted violence’ approach (JMCC, 1991; [Kaufman, 1990](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr27-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)), which was successfully promoted by the nationalist-led United National Command of the Uprising (UNCU). This is not to say that deadly violence did not occur, but rather that nationalist activists perceived the Intifada as national defiance against an unjust authority and as an effort to _shake off_ (hence Intifada) the occupation through civil disobedience. In addition, they believed that limited violence would better serve their goals, facilitate popular participation and garner world empathy with their struggle.

Not all PLO factions fully agreed with this line of reasoning, and a considerable portion of the Palestinian public was willing to employ more militant tactics ([Khalidi, 1988](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr28-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 504). Nevertheless, the UNCU was consistently the central leadership throughout most of the Intifada. To secure broad participation, it designated specific roles and activities, all rooted in the Palestinian primary frameworks: to women, the ‘creators of warriors’ and to witness and defuse ‘rites of violence’ ([Peteet, 1994](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr40-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)); to children, the ‘children of the stones’ or ‘sons of the _Fida'yyin_’; and to insurgents, the ‘sons of _Fida'yyin_’. Also, it avoided extreme demands that would overburden the population and reduce its ability to endure the struggle. The UNCU also made judicious efforts to glorify equally a variety of modes of action (e.g. _Sahmed_ and _Fida'y_). Male youths taking risks and coping with beatings and detentions were elements of masculinity embedded in the primary framework of the <subject>, expressions of fearlessness and assertiveness that were part of rites of passage to manhood ([Peteet, 1994](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr40-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 34).

While coordination between the nationalist movement and the Islamic Jihad was relatively successful (the result of past collaboration and Fatah's support of Jihad's operation) similar attempts with MB/Hamas brought less success. Throughout most of 1988, Hamas did not openly oppose the UNCU, despite its criticisms of the nationalists' tactics. Although its leaflets emphasized different styles, values and modes of action, there were frequent calls for unity to maintain the momentum of the Intifada, with a relatively small portion of calls for deadly violence ([Mishal with Aharoni, 1989](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr36-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x); [Paz, 1992](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr39-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)).<sup><a href="https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#fn5-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x" role="doc-noteref" id="body-ref-fn5-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x">5</a></sup>

Things began to change in the second half of 1988, most notably in the context of the Palestinian declaration of independence in November and, to a greater extent, with Arafat's subsequent speech in the UN expressing his willingness to accept a two-state-solution. Faced with a threat to its standing and influence within the movement, Hamas openly challenged the PLO, not only on the issue of who represents the Palestinian people – along with corresponding definitions of <subject> – but also, on the pace and tone of the uprising. In a leaflet (#33) issued immediately following Arafat's speech, Hamas called for ‘Struggle and confrontation on Sunday, December 25, memorializing the establishment of the Jihad groups in Palestine by the _shahid_ Abd al-Kader al-Husseini … Let the uprising continue in Jihad’.<sup><a href="https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#fn6-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x" role="doc-noteref" id="body-ref-fn6-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x">6</a></sup> Thus, while tension between the two groupings existed prior to Arafat's UN speech, expressed, _inter alia_, in different appropriations of images and heroic figures from the collective cultural stock,<sup><a href="https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#fn7-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x" role="doc-noteref" id="body-ref-fn7-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x">7</a></sup> the threat imposed on Hamas led it to react sharply. In the following months, it increasingly used acts of terrorism, such as the kidnapping and assassination of Israeli soldiers.

Facing Hamas' growing role in the uprising and its Islamic keying, as well as its greater reliance on violent tactics, Israel, already alarmed by the continuing and broadening conflict, reacted disproportionately and indiscriminately. Ironically, this led to the creation of an opportunity for Hamas, legitimizing its own particular keying of action. While at first the ‘restricted violence’ strategy was perceived to be successful in promoting the struggle _vis-à-vis_ outside third parties (e.g. the international community and Israeli public), growing signs that the Intifada was about to spill over to other arenas (notably inside Israel and Jordan) brought about a harsh Israeli policy aimed at suppressing the uprising by using brute force and live ammunition. Moreover, the hope that ‘restricted violence’ would influence the Israeli public was shattered as the 1988 national elections in Israel resulted in yet another Unity government with a strong right-wing orientation. Defense Minister Rabin's policy of massive military presence, extensive arrests, threats of mass deportation, administrative detention and extensive use of live ammunition only fueled the uprising further. It also led to media portrayals of Israel as an inhumane occupier and UN Security Council Resolution No. 605 condemning Israel's ‘merciless policy’ for controlling the Intifada.

In the face of still high numbers of deaths and injuries, and consequent harsher international criticism, Israel looked for ways to lower the profile of repression and minimize physical contact with the population (i.e. heavier reliance on collective punishment such as impositions of curfews, closures, sieges, etc.). The new policy for controlling the Intifada involved a systematic attempt by military forces to keep away from main streets and population centers, and to rely more on the use of covert modes of oppressive measures (JMCC, 1991, p. 31). This meant increased reliance on intelligence for tracing Palestinian individuals suspected to be the key activists responsible for sustaining the momentum of the uprising. The new policy's central facet was the large-scale and frequent use of undercover squads. Soldiers of special units named ‘Doovdevun’ (Cherry) and ‘Shimshon’ (Samson), disguised in traditional Arab garb and heavily armed, would travel around the territories in local cars (JMCC, 1991, p. 31). At times, they even tried to join crowds of stone throwers to identify the ‘hard-core’ activists.

The new policy proved successful in significantly lowering the Palestinian death toll. It succeeded also in creating confusion and distrust among Palestinians and increased the shootings of alleged collaborators with Israel. In addition to that, the rise of a right-wing government in Israel in June 1990, and a series of brutal attacks on Palestinians (e.g. the Rishon Le'Zion massacre), led to growing frustration and anger among the nationalists and infuriated the population. PLO hardliners (DFLP and PFLP activists) began to collaborate with Hamas, issuing joint leaflets calling for the use of deadly weapons and appealing for raids on Israel from Arab states. The strategy of ‘shaking off’ the occupation and ‘restricted violence’ shifted to ‘burning the ground under the feet of the occupation's army … until the troops of the occupation are killed in the streets’, as a UNCU leaflet issued following the massacre stated ([Litvak, 1991](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr32-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 259).

While our analysis focuses on the first Intifada, during the 1990s and towards the 2000 Intifada, the action frame was gradually re-keyed by both nationalist and Islamist activists to Islamic-based jihad. Arafat made efforts to appease the Islamist forces (e.g. releasing Hamas activists from prison) and stated repeatedly that he would march into Jerusalem with an army of _Shahids_. We can begin to understand this process with reference to several factors in the ongoing keying of ‘what to do’. On the <object's> side, we have the massacre at the Cave of the Patriarchs in Hebron on 25 February 1994 perpetrated by extreme right-wing zealot Baruch Goldstein, the repressive and provocative policy of the Netanyahu-led right-wing coalition, the provocative visit of Sharon to the Temple Mount, and the Mohamed a-Dura incident on 30 September 2000. On the <subject's> side, widespread discontent gathered over the Palestinian Authority's misjudgment, corruption and nepotism. In an interview given to the _Palestine Times_ in April 2000, Hamas spokesperson in Hebron Abdel Khaleq al-Natshe said: ‘The Palestinian Authority is our oppressor, but again we don't like to accentuate this too much, since our conflict is with Israel’. This stance was consistent with the grand scheme of Marwan Barghouti's Tanzim nationalists to rekindle another popular struggle, stating in March 2000: ‘Whoever thinks it is possible to reach a final status agreement through negotiation would better stop fantasizing. In these matters, we must engage in confrontations. We need tens of battles like the _al-Aqsa_\[i.e. Western Wall\] Tunnel’ (see [Harel and Isacharoff, 2004](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr18-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 48).<sup><a href="https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#fn8-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x" role="doc-noteref" id="body-ref-fn8-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x">8</a></sup> The convergence between the forces took shape in the form of the Nationalist and Islamist Front, formed in late 2000, and practically set the pace and tone of a more lethal and deadly cycle of contention labeled _al-Aqsa_ Intifada, denoting the religious ardor of the struggle against the Israeli-Jewish enemy.

## Concluding Discussion

In this article we have sought to develop a conceptual framework, one that expands on McAdam, Tarrow and Tilly's lead, for tracing the temporal sequence of how cultural and interpretative processes work through the major players in episodes of contention. Applied to the Chechen and Palestinian movements, culture and agency went hand in hand as primary frameworks were invoked yet variably applied and strategically modified, affecting perceptions of opportunity, threat and innovative collective action as political contexts shifted and collective interests changed among the players.

Our approach has, first, reintroduced Goffman's concepts of primary frameworks, frame rims and keying. Recognizing the cognitive foundation of primary frameworks helps capture how deep cultural patterns can influence the ongoing definition and redefinition of collective action. Second, drawing on Franzosi's semantic triplet, we have introduced the <subject> <verb> <object> distinctions into our analysis, based on the idea that interpretative processes can be profitably analyzed in terms of a basic story grammar of action. This analytical strategy builds on the non-controversial – in our judgment – assumption that all primary frameworks of contention will minimally have each of these elements in some basic way.

Third, we suggest that the usefulness of this framework is especially revealed by how it lays bare the internal relatedness and dynamism among these three elements – rather than each being fixed and independent of one another, lifted from a ‘cultural toolbox’ and applied strategically. Thus, there is a fundamental internal dynamic in the keying process by which (1) primary frameworks guide but are not deterministic, and (2) for each element, the variable keying of one limits the range of possibilities for the others.

Lastly, our analysis reveals that the keying process itself is a form of contentious politics. Again, this kind of contention is implicit in the _Dynamics of Contention_ enterprise, and within various studies of framing battles within movements (see [Snow, 2004](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr46-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x) for a summary), but our approach emphasizes that contention takes place not just over claims and strategy, the <verb>, but also over identity, the <subject>, and definitions of the <object>, the enemy. While the complexity of interpretative processes is implied by the inherent symbolic interactionism of framing theory, framing research does not capture systematically the dynamism among these basic components. We have in mind the following relationships:

•

Because <verb> and <object> are predicated on the <subject>, how the <subject> is defined or keyed will strongly guide the keying of <verb> and <object>. Our analysis suggests that the <subject> is _primus inter pares_ in the tripartite story grammar.

•

Because the action directed at the object is determined by the <verb>, its keying also affects the keying of the <object>.

•

Because the <object> is not passive, how the <subject> keys it will depend in part on the actions of the <object>.

•

Because the <object> is the recipient of the <verb's> actions, the <object's> actions will also affect the keying of the <verb>.

The _Dynamics of Contention_ framework offers several processes and mechanisms that are relevant to this analysis, most notably the general process of actor constitution (which itself is a ‘robust’ interpretative process–[McAdam _et al._, 2001](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr35-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x), p. 313) and its related mechanisms of identity shift for the <subject>, social appropriation and innovative action for the <verb>, and certification/decertification and category formation for the <object>. Our analysis offers several propositions in this respect, most notably, demonstrating the persistent role of primary frameworks in the keying process for all three elements. For example, among both Chechens and Palestinians, the <subject> was keyed in terms of Islamic struggle. This occurred not only because of primary frameworks, but also because of ‘framing battles’ with other contenders. Additionally, our analysis has shown that while causal mechanisms work in many directions, there is justification that further research pursues the primacy of some relationships over others. We have in mind the dependency of strategy on collective identity, as mentioned above, it being the predicate of the subject. Paralleling the emphasis that the new social movements perspective placed on collective identity ([Jasper, 1997](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr23-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x); [Polletta, 2006](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1111/j.1467-9248.2011.00927.x#bibr41-j-1467-9248-2011-00927-x)), but applying it provocatively in a political process analysis, our study suggests that definition of the subject – the movement's collective identity – is a hinge on which crucial elements of contention open and close, most notably, strategic decisions and perceptions of the target and of threat.

Finally, while the process of actor constitution captures the dynamic between the collective actor and the object, our tripartite schema is a more parsimonious way of showing how the target's actions (the <object>) affect not just ‘actor constitution’ but also the keying of ‘what must be done’ and its own keying by the <subject>. We have seen how Russia's overwhelming force radicalized Chechens and privileged those pushing for a more radical global jihad rim to the <verb>. Also, we have seen how Israel's draconian crackdown made the preferable definition of the <verb> of MB/Hamas more culturally resonant and, in turn, strengthened its role in the battle over defining the <subject>. McAdam _et al._'s formulation of actor constitution captures the dynamic relation of different mechanisms in multiple ways, but this process then needs to be linked with other interpretative/cultural processes regarding action strategy and perception of opportunity/threat.

## Biographies

**Hank Johnston** is Professor of Sociology at San Diego State University. His research focuses on nationalist movements, political mobilization in authoritarian regimes, framing theory and cultural elements of social movements. He has done research in Spain, the Baltic and Transcaucasian states of the former Soviet Union, Eastern Europe, Venezuela and, currently, Mexico. He is founding editor and publisher of _Mobilization: An International Quarterly_. His most recent books are _States and Social Movements_ (Polity, 2011), _Culture, Social Movements, and Protest_ (Ashgate, 2010), _Latin American Social Movements_ (with Paul Almeida, Rowman & Littlefield, 2008) and _Frames of Protest_ (with John Noakes, Rowman & Littlefield, 2005). He is editor of the _Mobilization_ series on ‘Protest, Social Movements, and Culture’ with Ashgate Publishers. Hank Johnston, Department of Sociology, San Diego State University, San Diego, CA 92182-4423, USA; email: [hank.johnston@sdsu.edu](mailto:hank.johnston@sdsu.edu)

**Eitan Y. Alimi** is Assistant Professor of Political Sociology at the Department of Political Science, the Hebrew University. He received his PhD in Sociology from Boston College in April 2004 and joined the department in summer 2007. His research interests include contentious politics and social movements, radicalization and political terrorism, and conflict resolution in comparative perspective. Recent publications include articles in _Political Studies_, _Comparative Politics_, _Theory and Society_, _British Journal of Political Science_, _International Journal of Press and Politics_, _Mobilization_, _International Political Science Review_, _Sociological Forum_, _Studies in Conflict and Terrorism_ and a book entitled _Israeli Politics and the First Palestinian Intifada_ (Routledge). Eitan Y. Alimi, Department of Political Science, Hebrew University, Jerusalem, Mount Scopus 91905, Israel; email: [alimien@012.net.il](mailto:alimien@012.net.il)
