The readings selected to approach Weber's sociology slightly defined as the "interpretative understanding of meaningfully oriented action" (p. 315), covers a broad scope of theoretical problems, categories and assumptions which are still being reproduced mainly by mainstream American political sociology and political science. The focus of these section of the syllabus was specifically pointed to the problem of meaning, social action, and power.

It is important what Weber understands by social action, in relation to the categories of power and meaning. The category of meaning is denoting the content imprinted as the orientation of every action, which is the meaning which acquires the anticipated or desired behavior of others inclusively considering the possibility of resistance. In that sense, an action cannot be social if it is not meaningfully intended to influence the behavior of other under certain imprinted orientation (pp. 327-329). Also, every action which can't be interpretable in that sense is not a social action, and "would be simply introduced at certain points into the interpretative understanding of motivational contexts" (p. 315).

Is important to pay attention to Whimster's editor footnote 12, when it's suggesting the term "meaning of rationality" instead than "instrumental rationality" pointing to its literal meaning as the end rationally intended, and its "clear contrast to the means of achieving" that end. Making that clarification, the distinction between the determinants of social action offered by Weber considered a meaning or instrumental rationality, which is directly related to Weber's concept of power, precisely in the employment of the expectations of the behavior of others as "a condition or means for the realization of one's own intended, premeditated aims and purposes" (p. 329). Social action, can be determined also by value rationality (oriented toward the prosecution of an intrinsic value or good independently of their success), affect (emotions or feelings), or tradition (habituation).

It's also relevant to emphasize the necessity for actual regularity or patterns of social actions repeated by certain actors with a typical intended meaning. For Weber, "[s]ociology is concerned with typologies of such modes of action, unlike
history, which concerns itself with causal imputation in respect of important,
fateful singular events" (p. 333).

For the discussion, I will consider first the selected readings to approach effect of the diffusion and change of ethical beliefs, particularly religious one's, over the trajectories of capitalist development. This point is markedly relevant for the interpretation of Weber's work because he is attempting to counter historical materialism stating that:

"*An economic ethic is not a straightforward 'function' of the organizational
forms of the economy, just as, conversely, an economic form cannot be
unambiguously derived from a religious ethic*" (p. 56).

For Weber the effects of Protestant ascetism diffusion among the growing middle-class have the outcome of expanding capital accumulation. The mechanism that Weber used to explain the effect of Protestant ascetism on accumulation of capital are mainly oriented to describe a particular behavior of rational consumption, reduced consumption, specially of luxury goods, while incentives the acquisition of "middle-class home" "clean and solid comfort" goods. Although, the economic effects on capital accumulation caused by the diffusion of certain ethical beliefs that influence the "way of life" of a growing "middle-class" are not causally explanatory.

Whimster's editor footnote 5 (p. 219) makes the distinction of what Weber refers to as ‘goods’. He reference to goods of the economic sphere in the normal sense, but the extension of the term goods is stretched to the spheres of “kinship groups, politics as well as economics, culture, the erotic, and the intellectual sphere [...]. Depending on the historical context, a good may be an inner value - love of poetry, or belief in God, or it can be an external acquisition - such as power or wealth or ostentation” (p. 219). But inclusively, considering this stretched definition of goods made by Weber, it doesn't excuse himself to identify the economic materiality of the causal mechanism which explain in the "economic sphere" the accumulation of capital by the diffusion of ascetic Protestantism on the way of life of the European and North American middle classes.

Weber stated that:

"*This worldly Protestant asceticism, as we may recapitulate up to this point,
acted powerfully against the spontaneous enjoyment of possessions; it
**restricted consumption, especially of luxuries**. On the other hand, it had the
**psychological effect of freeing the acquisition of goods from the inhibitions
of traditionalistic ethics**" (p. 27).

The discussion I want to point here is oriented to evaluate the validity of Weber's causal explanation of the effect of Protestant ascetism among the middle-classes and the development of capital accumulation. The limitation of the consumption of luxury goods, and the rational acquisition of goods in general is contrary to the interest and intentions of the capitalists. Why the effect of restriction of consumption of luxuries and a rational consumption of goods in the way of life of the middle-classes motivated by Protestant ascetism increase or develop capital accumulation? 

In the following lines I will argue that the reproduction of capital is negatively affected by the contraction or restriction in the consumption of material "goods" in general and specifically of luxuries, particularly by the growing middle-class. 
The first reason is because the agency of the capitalist is never oriented to save money; it is completely oriented, rationally intended toward the increasing of its profits. And never capital accumulation is produced by the savings of any capitalists or middle-class, the only way in which capitalists accumulate capital is by profit. 

The second reason is because the scale of capital accumulation increased with the productivity of labor developed by the "embryonic" industrial bourgeoisies would be favored in their circuit of capital accumulation by the consumption of the commodities produced, not by the restriction of its consumption. Particularly, capital accumulation would increase with the consumption of luxury "goods" among the middle classes because the production of those goods implied a higher magnitude of capital accumulated if those are sold or consumed in a larger scale. In which manner, the capitalist increase its capital and increments its productive investment. Not by the contrary as Weber stated in the second part of his argument:

"*When the limitation of consumption is combined with this release of acquisitive activity, the inevitable practical result is obvious: **accumulation of capital through ascetic compulsion to save**. The restraints which were imposed upon the consumption of wealth naturally served to increase it by **making possible the productive investment of capital**" (p. 28).

The savings of middle class homes promoted by the Protestant ascetism "way of life" don't explain the development of the process of capital accumulation after the middle age, it's much more empirically probable that capital accumulation realized by the capitalists of those years mainly associated with Italian-Dutch-Spanish global hegemony transitions was caused by the consumption of luxury goods by the upper classes, feudal and capitalists upper classes, made possible because of the extraction of gold and silver by the means of colonization as a method of original and primitive accumulation and it's importation to the European metropolis as money-capital that will finance not only the consumption of the upper classes (feudal or bourgeoisies), but the formation of the European modern states and the following expansion of capitalism since the XVI century.
