# 1. Long and short bio for IPH

Since my early steps in academic institutions at the beginning of the 2000s in Peru, my intellectual curiosity merged with social movements research and activism while my formal scholar education is subsumed under the development of episodes of struggle as the October insurrection of 2019 in Quito (Ecuador), my interpretation of social movements theory is entangled within contexts connoted by episodes of contention. I grounded my research on anti-mining struggles in Los Andes, where the indigenous-cholo peasantry organizations survived and developed under the coloniality of power, capital, and knowledge, but not in a passive manner as the history of indigenous, anticolonial and anti-imperialist resistances showed widely. In this sense, my formation as a Marxist researcher in historical political sociology is aligned with the developing concrete needs of the Andean and Latin American antimining movement.

The development of an indigenous-cholo peasant antimining movement reflects the exacerbation of the contradictions between social reproduction and capital reproduction between two antagonistic social classes: the indigenous-cholo peasantry struggling against mining dispossession and destruction of land and water, and the international banks states capitalists trusts finance capital reproduction through its investment on the mining circuit of accumulation.
The effective instrumentation of socialism, environmental politics, and identity politics in antimining struggles by the indigenous-cholo peasantry organizations, have reached a point of maturation in which the strengthen of their organizations is transformed into political power not only by transgressive contentious politics but also by contained (institutionalized) contentious politics.

I joined the IHC Public Humanities Graduate Fellows Program to develop a collaborative and multidisciplinary research project in partnership with antimining social movements organizations, public scholars, NGOs, and fundraising organizations, oriented to the empowerment of the indigenous-cholo struggles against mining dispossession and destruction of land and water. My research on antisystemic movements seeks to contribute to fill the theoretical gap between Marxism and historical political sociology, by recognizing the class struggle of the Andean indigenous-cholo peasantry against the finance capital reproduction of the top international banks states capitalists’ trusts operating by the circuit of mining accumulation. 

By a public humanist approach to antimining movements research, my participation in the IHC Public Humanities Graduate Fellows Program pursues the building of intelligible and useful theory for a broader public than the audience of specialized-scholars already empowered by their knowledge. In contrast, I work to develop theory which will be instrumented by the organizations rooted in the defense of water and land from the dispossession and destruction caused by the large-scale-mining total annihilation of biodiversity and of the conditions for present and future life in the territories sacrificed by mining companies, at the paramos and punas of the highlands, and in the Amazonian forests of Los Andes. I'm committed in develop research projects or grants application with direct activism components, and building networks and opportunities oriented to improve the political, institutional and economic resources of the indigenous-cholo peasantry organizations of Los Andes.

October: Journal of Marxist Social Sciences and Political Economy

The development of critical theory at the present historical juncture is an instrument of struggle. Political sociology as a social science understanding the built of power from the bottom to the top, was excerpted from political economy. The critical power of historical political sociology was constrained by its divorce with the multiverse of Marxism, with differentiated ratios between the mainstream and the radical approaches for social change that are an integral part of it.

Nevertheless, as the events presented, the most critical theories of globalization, world-system research, contentious politics and social movement studies, as the mainstream branches of historical political sociology lacked, denied or rejected an exposure to a Marxist critique or theoretical bridge for advanced sophistication.
