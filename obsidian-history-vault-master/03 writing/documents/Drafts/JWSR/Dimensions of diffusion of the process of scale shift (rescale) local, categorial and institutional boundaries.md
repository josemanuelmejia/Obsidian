
The process of scale shift has been addressed in the literature on social movements by Alimi (2011), Tarrow (2010), and Tarrow and McAdam (2005), among others, keeping unattended the problematization of the limits that define the "scale" of the political contention, or – rather – avoided theoretical sophistication in relation to it.
Thus, it is common for social movement studies to be associated with a one-dimensional approach to socio-spatial relations that Jessop, Brenner, and Jones (2008, 391) label as "methodological territorialism": manifested in the reduction of social space to territorial limits, usually defined from a state-centered perspective in relation to the borders or jurisdictional circumscriptions of a given level of government or political regime. 
This approach, which avoids problematizing the local, categorial and institutional limits of scale shift by reducing its diffusion to the territorial dimension, has been useful for research on social movements and transnational political conflict (Imig and Tarrow 2001; Bandy and Smith 2005; Della Porta et al. 2006; Della Porta and Tarrow 2005b; 2011; Tarrow 2005; 2010; 2001; Tarrow and McAdam 2005). However, its effectiveness is much less when applied to the diffusion of collective action in or towards social spaces which edges (Abbott 1995; Tilly 2004) do not necessarily correspond to the territorial limits associated with political-administrative circumscriptions such as: sub-national, national, international or transnational.
Faced with this trend, Jessop, Brenner, and Jones (2008, 396) argued for overcoming one-dimensional approaches to social space through the inclusion of at least two socio-spatial dimensions of the four identified in the TPSN analysis framework: territory, place, scale, and networks; in such a way that their connotation as operational structuring principles denote fields delimited by some combination of those socio-spatial dimensions.

In this sense, they point out that the dimensions of territory, place, scale and networks can denote (i) in themselves, the socio-spatial product of their own principles of structuring; (ii) the structuring principles (or causal mechanisms) that operate on fields configured by other socio-spatial dimensions; and (iii) **the socio-spatial fields structured by the operation of other structuring principles**.

Thus, as Jessop, Brenner, and Jones (2008) point out, the recent state of the art of research that addresses social space tends to theorize by combining at least two of the dimensions specified in the TPSN approach:43 territory, place, scale, and networks. In this sense, following the polymorphic approach to socio-spatial theory proposed by Jessop, Brenner, and Jones (2008) and considering that, for the aforementioned authors, the research program of political contest44 is one of the fields with the best possibilities of following a polymorphic approach to social space (Jessop, Brenner, and Jones 2008, 397-98); it is decided to maintain the local, categorial and institutional dimensions present in the definition of the process of change of scale (McAdam, Tarrow, and Tilly 2005, 367) as defining the social space of the conflict.
By change of scale we mean a change in the number and level of coordinated contentious actions that leads to a more generalized contest that includes a wider range of actors and that serves as a bridge between their diverse claims and identities [...]. The vast majority of contentious actions never go beyond the local, categorial or institutional context in which they first appear. But in the main episodes of political strife, almost by definition, there must be at least a certain degree of change of scale. to appreciate that there are new incidents that follow the outbreak of the conflict; new actors who adhere to forms of conflict ventured by their predecessors; more general claims and identities that crystallize from the interactions that occur between the contenders (McAdam, Tarrow, & Tilly 2005, 367).

In this sense, the territorial dimension defines the scale of an episode of political conflict depending on the territory in which the dynamics of the conflict are disseminated. Local boundaries correspond to the territorial constituencies that define the jurisdiction of a state, government, or political regime: sub-national, national, international, or transnational. Likewise, the categorical dimension corresponds to the size and heterogeneity of the network of relationships that make up the challengers in the campaigns of contention.

The more binding or influential the results of the conflict over the activities, resources and interests of a greater number of actors, the greater its escalation. Likewise, the greater or broader the demands, claims, interests, resources, identities, actors involved or affected in a political conflict, the greater its escalation. The diffusion of political contest through the institutional dimension does not only refer to the change in the object of the claim that goes from sub-national to national government and even to the transnational level – as is well evidenced by the Europeanization of the political contest at the end of the twentieth century and the beginning of the twenty-first century in the countries immersed in the European Union (Imig and Tarrow 2001). The escalation of the contest can also be evidenced in the linking of government agencies initially alien to the decision-making process against which the challengers execute their claims.
