Good afternoon, at this opportunity I will offer to you a short presentation on the Protest Events Analysis method. {slide1}
First of all, we requiere some agreement on a minimal operative definition of what a protest event is.
A protest in this regard, is conceptualized as a kind of collective action featured by: (i) a public gathering of more than 10 participants, (ii) executing some disruptive performance, (iii) oriented to the satisfaction of a collective claim, (iv) by a political decision. {slide2}
This minimal concept of a protest event will operate as a unit of observation for the building of a catalog of protests dataset prone to be analyzed quantitively and qualitatively for the study of short, medium and long term political processes.
Second, we have to select the sources for the recognition and register of every protest event executed under the period, geography, and/or field of struggle of our interest.
In this session we will use as an example the Political Protests Dataset of Peru developed by Prof. Moises Arce (Tulane University), in which I worked on applying the PEA method using as data sources three Peruvian national scope newspapers (El Comercio, Expreso, and La República) for the period 2016-2020.
Third, after scan the news on which every protest event appears reported by each of the three newspapers, we have to analyze and merge the registered protest events according to the following elemental six variables:
a. subject
b. action
c. claim
d. target
e. date
f. place
Let me clarify, minimally, what are we looking for by registering this variables for the analysis of protest events. 
a. The subject (actor) allow us to identify the proper collective names, or instead the social category, used in the labeling or self-recognition of a group of protesters in collective action. For example: the Nightwatch Peasants organizations (Rondas Campesinas), or the National Unique Union of Educational Workers (SUTEP).
b. The action (verb) attempts to capture the set of performances of struggle by which the collective action of those protesters was executed. For example: roadblocks, strikes, hunger-strikes, takeovers, riots, marches. 
c. The claim (demand) capture the specific justification and rationality of the protest as a political resource for the pursue of collective interests. As for example: the retirement of mining companies, free public education, wages rises, or the reversion of a public policy, law, or other political decision taken.
d. The target (object) of the protest is intended to register the organization challenged in their interests by the protest, and usually involve directly and/or indirectly a political decision or state intervention by repression.
e. The date is necessary to identify the sequences of protest events and later measure the duration of sets of connected protests which we can recognize as campaigns of protest as durable sequences of protests executed by different challengers for a consistent and concrete collective claim.
f. The place is also required to stablish the territorial scale of a protest and an imprescindible context variable. As protest can be coordinated for being executed on different places at the same day, each one is registered and counted as a differentiated protest. For example: a general strike which involves roadblocks in several subnational locations, in that case each protest event placed on different territories are considered as separated.
Fourth, as we collected data for at least the six variables already specified from three different sources (newspapers of national scope), to avoid double or triple counting of events is necessary to merge the registered information of the same protest event reported in two or three of the sources (following our example catalog), creating an aggregated data entry for a protest event if the place and date variables are the same and the information registered is complementary and non-contradictory.
Fifth, once the dataset of protest events have a reliable counting as a result of the aggregation and merge of reported protest events described in step 4. We can develop an analysis of the sets of coordinated connected sequences of protest events by the recognition of the revindication of the same consistent claim by similar or different challengers executing similar or differentiated performances of struggle on different places at sequential dates.
