

  

Hello and welcome to the UCSB Sociology PhD program! We are so excited to have you in our department and to have the opportunity to collaborate with you, take classes with you, socialize with you, and all around get to know you over the next 5+ years! 2020 was the first year, at least as far back as we know, that graduate students in the department put together a “Soc Grad How To” guide for incoming students, and we have maintained it since. This guide is written by grads for grads -- we aren’t experts on the program requirements or academia (that’s why we’re here!), but we want to share what we’ve learned along the way. This guide does not explain program requirements: it is for all the life things in between. You should ALWAYS look at YOUR [cohort's handbook](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1euAoSnTFDCy-9MzmPNPaCYZB5x5z81DJ6jaSemKPExk/edit#heading=h.p59u6fl3gh5p) (we will link the 2023-2024 handbook once it is posted) and verify department policy / program requirements with the Grad Program Advisor, Monica Gascon, or the Director of Graduate Studies, Sarah Thebaud. 

  

There are a lot of things in grad school that no one really explains to you. This isn’t on purpose: usually people further along in academia just don’t remember what it was like to be new, so they don’t even know what you don’t know! In any case, these tidbits of knowledge are immensely helpful to know going in. Hopefully, this guide will help you “get in the groove” of grad school, serve as a resource throughout your first year, and save you from some confusion. It is by no means exhaustive or an authority on the matter: some sections may not align with your experience, and some suggestions might even contradict one another because we crowd-sourced this info, and not all grads have had the same experience. Take what resonates with you, and feel free to disregard what doesn’t.

  

This “Soc Grad How To” is like Wikipedia: it is community-sourced, but is also public, and anyone on the internet can read it if they have the link. It is pretty informal, but information should always be linked to sources if possible so folks can go read more and double check info. Feel free to add comments and suggestions as you see fit, but remember that your name will be associated with them. If you are an incoming grad and have a question about something or would like something further clarified, please leave a comment. If you are a current grad student and want to contribute or clarify something, please feel free to add it. Settings allow editing as suggestions so that the Sociology Graduate Student Association (SGSA) can keep track of changes and approve them before they are officially added. If you want to add something without your name being attached to a suggestion, please email [socgradassociation@gmail.com](mailto:socgradassociation@gmail.com).

  

Things change -- the information we wrote in August may no longer be accurate in November. Double check information as needed. If you see something that needs to be updated, please do so kindly, as this is a community resource, and we are all trying our best to keep it up-to-date. Again, you should ALWAYS verify department policy and program requirements with Monica Gascon (Grad Program Advisor) or Sarah Thebaud (Director of Graduate Studies).

Lastly, our hope for this document has always been to update it on a continual basis, but that more substantial additions and amendments will be made at the end of each year by the most recent cohort of graduate students. You are the ones who will be most knowledgeable about what is important to know/do/think about during your first year. By working on this guide and then sending it to the incoming students, you will also be able to establish a connection with them before they arrive, alleviate some of their anxiety, and make them feel welcome in the department, as we have hopefully done for you!

  

All the best,

Summer 2023 Sociology Graduate Student Association (SGSA) Board 

Emily Fox

Timothy Perez

# Land Acknowledgment

  

It may not come as a surprise to you, but UCSB is on stolen land (more on [public universities and land grabbing](https://www.landgrabu.org/), including the UC system; also check out [whose land you’re on at this moment](https://native-land.ca/)). The original stewards of the land we now call Santa Barbara County-- including Goleta, Santa Barbara, Isla Vista, UCSB, and Montecito (as well as further up and down the coast) -- are the [Chumash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chumash_people), composed of three bands that still live here with us: the [Barbareño Chumash](http://www.bbcindians.com/), the [Coastal Band](https://coastalbandofthechumashnation.weebly.com/), and the [Santa Ynez Chumash](https://www.santaynezchumash.org/). The University, and by extension, any and all non-natives living at UCSB, currently occupy the land of the Coastal Band of the Chumash and the Barbareno Chumash, which historically was a place of dwelling where fishing and foraging were the way of life next to the Pacific. It can’t be stressed enough that these are living and vibrant cultures, the Chumash still live here! If you aren’t indigenous, you are a settler on this land! Be respectful of the fact that you’re on stolen land. 

  

If you want to get more involved with indigenous issues on campus there is the American Indian Student Association, the American Indian Graduate Student Association, and the American Indian and Indigenous Collective Academic Council. To get connected with these groups, visit the [American Indian and Indigenous Cultural Resource Center (AIICRC)](http://eop.sa.ucsb.edu/aicrc/).

# 1. Pre-Arrival

- ## What to know before you get to SB
    

- ### Lay of the land![](https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/3d-zoj92_X4Muf3YKyjBKqYVTJn2Wxss10wLBbeaA0dik4_l_3UTlwyHg8WVWr35Hq8eE2JxrKyHkLs28P1i1cn7tW2GA0csTAzjmerqkEnLG8uPIaNbZ1vECXjX4Jyw8jIOlTzppBq2TF_BodsS4r4)
    

- It may come as a surprise, but UCSB (red pin on map) isn’t actually in the city of Santa Barbara, which is about a 20 minute drive east from campus. UCSB is nestled between the city of Goleta and the beach town, Isla Vista. 
    
- Santa Barbara is a rich / resort beach city about 2 hours north of LA. There’s a pier, beaches, shops, restaurants, and parks. More on this later!
    
- Montecito is even richer ( Oprah, Ellen DeGeneres, and Prince Harry and Megan Markle live there) and is to the east of Santa Barbara. If you get a chance, take a drive here and check out the houses and landscaping. 
    
- Goleta is a small town west of Santa Barbara composed of mostly working class and Latinx inhabitants. There is one main strip about a 5 minute drive from campus where you can find a Chinese market, a Mexican market, good tacos, a single dive bar, and a whole lot of car mechanics.
    
- Isla Vista (locally known by its initials, IV) is the town right next to campus and south of Goleta. It is mostly occupied by undergraduates and the exorbitantly priced housing that they are required to pay for. Once you get to the 6800 block you start to find more residential houses. The main attraction is a handful of establishments that serve alcohol, and the food spots that serve drunk students… and the beaches… more on all this later.

- ## Picking classes 
    

- ### Quarter system
    

- Most UCs, including UCSB, are on a quarter system. Fall quarter goes late September to Winter Break (December); Winter Quarter is January to Spring Break (March); and Spring Quarter goes from March to Summer Break (late June). There are Summer sessions as well, but no grad courses are offered, so you might work on your own research, find another job, or apply to TA to make some money during the summer. You can find the specific dates for each quarter [here](https://registrar.sa.ucsb.edu/calendars/calendars-deadlines/academic-calendars/academic-calendar-menu). 
    
- Because our quarter terms are only 10 weeks of instruction (and one week of finals), classes will seem to fly by if you are used to the semester system. So make sure you don’t get lulled into a false sense of security at the beginning of the quarter and think you have a lot of time to do your final papers/projects: before you know it, it will be week 9. The up side of the quarter system is you rarely get bored of a class and you get to try out different courses and meet more professors and peers. 
    

- ### What to keep in mind when making a schedule
    

- Check out the section “Simple Breakdown of What Each Year Looks Like,” but here’s what you need to know to pick classes your first quarter:
    
- In general, you will have to take Professional Development and Sociological Theory A during your first quarter. This leaves one or two classes for you to pick. If you’d like to take a slightly less demanding first quarter, you can take one class in addition to the required ones. If you feel up to it, you can feasibly take two additional classes because the Professional Development seminar is very minimal work. Many grads would suggest that you take methods classes during your first year so that you have the tools you need to start your Masters thesis research by your second year (keep in mind that Field Methods is a two-quarter sequence). Feel free to also look outside the department, such as in Feminist Studies, Black Studies, Chicanx Studies, Anthropology, Global Studies, etc for classes, but know that you will have to petition for them to count toward your degree requirements.
    
- For Winter and Spring quarters of your first year, most grads recommend 12 credit hours (or less), which is 3 classes. The current minimum is 8 credit hours. 
    
- After your first year, most grads start TAing, which takes up a lot of time.  Taking 1 course (4 credit hours) + TAing (also counts at 4 credit hours) + working on your own research is a quire doable but still demanding, especially if you have anything else that limits your time and energy (parenting, chronic illness, another job on campus, etc.) Any more than 2 classes + TAing + your own research is going to be extremely demanding.
    

- ## Mindset
    

- A lot of success in grad school involves having a mindset that helps you succeed. Alas, many of the mindsets that served us well in our education thus far are less helpful in grad school and some things may be counterproductive. Sadly, by the time most of us find a mindset that works well in grad school we have forgotten what our mindset was before, so it can be difficult to articulate those changes to newer students. As mentioned earlier, a good portion of this guide is our attempt to articulate those things that we have picked up through our experience in grad school. 
    
- Grad School is a Job - Grad school is not school. It is training and professionalization as a researcher. This means that you are not a student in the sense that you are being taught, but you are a student in the sense that you are still learning. The responsibility for your success is placed almost exclusively on you, which means you need to create and maintain your own momentum (we have several tips on doing this later). The department treats each grad student like an investment. They want us to succeed and do well because that reflects well on the department and faculty. This is not entirely selfish however as ideally this means you are celebrated and get the credit when you succeed. More importantly, everyone wants you to succeed, especially your committee members.
    
- Your committee is everything
    

- Your committees (one for your Masters and one for your PhD) is the group of faculty that you are responsible for convening to mentor and guide you through your degrees. See definition of committee in Grad School & UCSB Lingo section.
    
- The department is a huge part of grad school, but the department doesn’t grant you a degree or sign your paperwork. Your committee does. Furthermore, while classes and seminars are great, the vast majority of your training will come from your committee, specifically your chair. Because of this it is very important that you choose your committee wisely and strategically and to develop a good working relationship with them. 
    
- A common mistake in grad school is waiting for someone to tell you exactly what your next step should be. You’ll find that for many faculty, their style is to give you advice not specific directions. They are unlikely to keep you to very specific interim deadlines for say, when you should complete your interviews or get transcripts written. If you’re someone who needs a lot of external accountability, a peer accountability group and calendar can be very helpful.
    

- Abandon perfectionism and completionism
    

- Several traits that serve people well in undergrad and get us to grad school become self-destructive now that we are grad students, particularly being a completionist (the desire to finish everything, i.e. read every word in the assigned book) and perfectionist. Good science is collaborative and this is especially true with sociology. Growing comfortable presenting your work before it meets your standard of “perfect” or “done” will greatly benefit you and your work. 
    
- Your life will get a lot easier once you lose the emotional attachment to the concept of being right. The hallmark of a good scientist is the ability to change your mind when presented with new information. This is both difficult and painful and even saying it seems callous. Still, the sooner you can start doing this the better you will do, both with work and your mental health.
    
- Part of being in academia is rejection -- your grant proposals, article submissions, job market rejections, etc. WILL get rejected. Notice we didn’t write “you will be rejected,” but rather “your grant proposals.” It’s hard, but try not to take these personally. It’s part of the process, and you always learn something along the way.
    

- Negative feedback is good
    

- Feedback will always sound negative, especially if you have the mindset of “if I did it right I will get only positive feedback.” Professors will always have things to say about your work and how it could be improved.  
    
- You will notice how a lot of grad school is training you to be able to critique top quality published research. This is a very good skill, but that same hyper critical eye can all too easily turn to your own work and destroy your confidence. This is especially true when others, particularly committee members critique your work just as we critique other scholarship in grad seminars. There is one very important thing to remember: If someone critiques your work it means they think it is worthy of critical thought and feedback.

# 2. Arrival/Living in CA

- ## What to know/do after you get to California if you are moving from another U.S. state
    

- During your first year, you are charged non-resident tuition (aka out-of-state tuition), but the department pays this extra 5k per quarter on your behalf. However, if you do not establish CA residency within the first year, you will be charged non-resident tuition your second year as well, and the department will no longer cover this extra expense. In order to not wind up with a hefty bill, make sure that you do these things as soon as possible!
    
- ### Switch address on bank info, insurance, loans, etc.
    

- Switching your mailing address with your bank may be the easiest step, and it will help you obtain more complicated things like a driver’s license, so do that as soon as you can. Mail from your bank is often considered a reliable proof of residence.
    
- Consider if you have any other things that you need to switch your mailing address for: phone service, health insurance (if you are on your parent’s or partner’s insurance instead of UC SHIP insurance), auto insurance, etc.
    

- ### Voter registration
    

- You can register to vote in CA online [here](https://www.sos.ca.gov/elections/voter-registration/). They will send you a postcard to confirm your address, which can also be used as proof of address to get your driver’s license. 
    

- ### Drivers license
    

- If you already have a driver license from another state, you’ll need to get a CA license with your new address. You’ll want to get a REAL ID, which requires a few more documents but is a federally-recognized form of identification, meaning that you can use it for air travel. Unless you want to spend half a day waiting at the DMV, schedule an appointment ahead of time. Here is a checklist of the [documents](https://www.dmv.ca.gov/portal/driver-licenses-identification-cards/real-id/how-do-i-get-a-real-id/real-id-checklist/) you will need for your application/appointment. Make sure to check the DMV website for up-to-date COVID policies. 
    
- Follow these steps (taken from [here](https://www.dmv.ca.gov/portal/driver-licenses-identification-cards/driver-licenses-dl/)) to get the ball rolling on a CA License:
    
- Complete a [Driver License or Identification (DL/ID) card application](https://www.edl.dmv.ca.gov/apply/choose-language).
    
- Visit a [DMV office,](https://www.dmv.ca.gov/portal/locations/field-offices/) where you will:
    

- Provide your social security number (SSN).
    
- Prove your identity with an [acceptable identity document](https://www.dmv.ca.gov/portal/file/federal-non-compliant-dl-id-card-documents-list-pdf/) (original or certified copy) for either a [Federal Non-Compliant](https://www.dmv.ca.gov/portal/file/federal-non-compliant-dl-id-card-documents-list-pdf/) or [REAL ID Compliant](https://www.dmv.ca.gov/portal/driver-licenses-identification-cards/real-id/how-do-i-get-a-real-id/real-id-checklist/) DL/ID Card. Your current name needs to match the name on the identity document (see the FAQs below for more information).
    
- Present [acceptable residency documents](https://www.dmv.ca.gov/portal/file/residency-documents-list-pdf/) (if you have never had a California DL/ID).
    
- Pay the nonrefundable [application fee](https://www.dmv.ca.gov/portal/licensing-fees/) (application and fee valid for 12 months).
    
- Have your thumbprint scanned.
    
- Pass a [vision exam](https://www.dmv.ca.gov/portal/fast-facts/vision-standards-ffdl-14/).
    
- Have your photo taken.
    
- Pass the [knowledge test(s](https://www.dmv.ca.gov/portal/preparing-for-knowledge-and-drive-tests/)). You are allowed three attempts to pass each of the required knowledge tests. If you fail the same test three times, your application is no longer valid and you will need to reapply. To allow for sufficient testing time, we do not administer knowledge tests within 30 minutes of closing.
    

- ### Vehicle registration (if you have a car registered in another state)
    

- Here is the [information for registering your vehicle in California](https://www.dmv.ca.gov/portal/vehicle-registration/new-registration/registering-a-vehicle-purchased-from-a-dealer/) and the documents that you will need. Similar to obtaining a driver’s license, make an appointment ahead of time if possible. 
    
- A [smog inspection](https://www.dmv.ca.gov/portal/vehicle-registration/smog-inspections/) is required for registration unless your car is very new, very old, or electric (check website). 
    
- Make sure to also check the [insurance requirements](https://www.dmv.ca.gov/portal/vehicle-registration/insurance-requirements/)
    

- ### CA residency
    

- You’ll need all of the above to demonstrate to the university that you now live in and intend to stay in California. After you’ve acquired all of these things, now you get to sit back and enjoy living in California until the summer after your first year, when you will submit a [Statement of Legal Residence](https://registrar.sa.ucsb.edu/fees-residency/residency/residency-for-tuition-purposes) (by [August 1](https://registrar.sa.ucsb.edu/fees-residency/residency/uc-residence-policy)) and upload supporting documents (by Sept 15). Make sure to glance at this [info about residency](https://my.sa.ucsb.edu/catalog/Current/FeesFinAid/EstablishingResidencyforFeePurposes.aspx) before then to ensure that you are following all the requirements, such as not leaving the state for more than 6 weeks during the first year of residency.

[Financial Aid office can be found here](https://www.finaid.ucsb.edu/), and [FAFSA information and forms here](https://studentaid.gov/h/apply-for-aid/fafsa).

- ## Dictionary of grad school, academy, and UCSB lingo
    

- ### Graduate student roles / funding
    

- #### ASE (Academic Student Employee)
    

- This is the legal/administrative term for grad and undergrad students who are employed by the university to conduct teaching and instruction duties (TAs, Teaching Associates, tutors, and readers). ASEs are unionized with [UAW 2865](https://uaw2865.org/).
    

- #### “TA” (teaching assistant) 
    

- Usually graduate students but sometimes upper level undergraduates who assist an instructor of record by grading and sometimes leading sections. TAs are paid by the university and are unionized with [UAW 2865](https://uaw2865.org/). TAs should be working a max of 20 hours a week/220 hours a quarter-- see definition for “UAW 2865 / union” and section on “TAing” under “Keys to Success in Grad School” for more info. Most soc grads are TAs after their first year unless they find outside funding (grant, fellowship, GSR, etc). In addition to being paid for your work, the university must also cover for your health insurance, tuition, and some fees when you work in this position.
    

- #### Instructor of Record 
    

- This is either a professor, lecturer, or post-MA graduate student teaching associate who is in charge of creating and leading the class. They write the syllabus, coordinate with their TAs, and give the lectures. They are called instructors of record because they are in charge of recording and submitting final grades for students.
    

- #### Teaching Associate 
    

- Graduate students who have completed their Masters can work as teaching associates, which means they are the instructor of record. This position pays a little more than a TA because it has more responsibilities, but considering that this job is basically creating a running an entire course, the pay increase really doesn’t come close to balancing out with the increased workload. Teaching Associates are paid by the university and are unionized with [UAW 2865](https://uaw2865.org/). In addition to being paid for your work, the university must also cover for your health insurance, tuition, and some fees when you work in this position.
    

- #### Reader 
    

- These are grad or undergrad students who are hired to assist a professor or TA in grading only. They are paid hourly, so you generally shouldn’t rely on this position for your funding, but you might do on top of TAing or being a GSR to make a bit more money. Readers are paid by the university and are unionized with [UAW 2865](https://uaw2865.org/). The university must cover health insurance for all readers who are employed at 25% or above. If that reader is a graduate student, the university must also cover tuition and some fees.
    

- #### “GSR” (Graduate Student Researcher)
    

- This is the legal/administrative term for grad students who are employed by the university to conduct research (GSRs and Research Assistants). GSRs are unionized with [UAW 2865](https://uaw2865.org/) as of 2022! Before 2022, they were not unionized, meaning that they did not have a work contract to limit the max number of work hours per week or day, provide job security, or set a protocol for addressing any workplace issues like harassment from the professor they worked for, late pay, etc. 
    
- It is actually possible to be an undergraduate student who is a graduate student researcher (yes, I am also rolling my eyes at the terrible word choice of this term), but that’s getting into the minutiae of the union contract, and you probably don’t need that.
    
- In addition to being paid for your work, the university must also cover for your health insurance, tuition, and some fees when you work in this position.
    
- GSRs are much more common in STEM fields than in the social sciences and humanities. GSR positions are associated with an individual professor who wants to use some of their grant money to employ a student who will assist with the professor’s research project. 
    

- #### Fellowship
    

- A fellowship is like a scholarship in that it’s “free money” awarded to you without you needing to do a specific job in return -- just be a student and do your research. Fellowships can either come from the department/school or from an outside source ([National Science Foundation](https://www.nsfgrfp.org/), [Ford Foundation](https://sites.nationalacademies.org/PGA/FordFellowships/index.htm), etc.). When you are awarded a fellowship from an outside source, they pay you a stipend as well as pay the school a cut--this, as well as the notoriety, is part of the reason the school encourages students to get fellowships. When you are on fellowship through the department your first year, your health insurance, tuition, and most fees are covered. This varies with other fellowships so make sure to check what fellowships cover when you apply. Your chair should be able to help you with this process, and make sure that you communicate with Monica as soon as you start to consider applying because often there is paperwork that she also has to do.
    

- #### Grants 
    

- The distinction between fellowships and grants is sometimes a bit murky, but in general you can think “fellowships go to people, grants go to projects.” This means that a fellowship is awarded to you because you seem like a promising scholar, while a grant is awarded to you because your project seems promising or noteworthy. This distinction is important when applying because grant applications generally require much more detail about your proposed project. The logistics of how someone gets paid with a grant are also complex. Make sure to research this more in depth when applying for grants. Again, it is part of your chair's responsibilities to help you with these kinds of things, so you should feel empowered to ask them for assistance.

- ### COLA / UCSB4COLA
    

- During the year of 2019-2020, graduate students across the UC rallied around a campaign for a “Cost of Living Adjustment” (COLA), which started at UC Santa Cruz after they felt the TA union UAW2865 did not adequately consider their needs as a small campus in an area with skyrocketing rent. They argued that their wages were not sufficient to cover their cost of living, specifically their rent, which has grown exponentially in California in recent years. By the end of the Winter 2020 quarter, grads at all UC campuses were organizing around COLA, and grads at UCSC, UCSB, UCSD, UC Davis, and UC Berkeley were engaged in a wildcat strike (a strike that is organized by workers on the ground and is not sanctioned by their union). UCSB was the second UC campus to launch a COLA campaign, and the second campus to begin striking. However, with the beginning of COVID, strike actions no longer seemed feasible, and all strikes ended. During COVID, UCSB4COLA directed its energy toward mutual aid.
    
- History: The COLA movement started at UC Santa Cruz in September of 2019. Graduate students at UC Santa Cruz gave notice to their university administration that they wanted a cost of living adjustment of $1,412 a month to address their rising housing costs and overall cost of living. After exhausting official channels and other means for communicating their concerns, they announced a grading strike on December 9, 2019. In response to intimidation and threats of discipline from UC Santa Cruz, they are engaged in a full graduate student strike as of February 10th. From the [first days of the strike](https://payusmoreucsc.com/monday-strike-update/), graduate students faced an excessive police presence (including police officers brought in from across the Bay Area and other UC campuses), resulting in arrests and injuries to peaceful student protestors. On the evening of February 14th, [faculty](https://payusmoreucsc.com/from-admin-graduate-student-strike-update/) and [graduate students](https://payusmoreucsc.com/from-admin-confidential-personnel-matter/) received letters from UC Santa Cruz Executive Vice Chancellor Lori Kletzer which threatened mass dismissal of teaching assistants who failed to submit grades, end the strike and return to work by February 21st. It later came out that UC was using [military surveillance tech](https://www.vice.com/en_us/article/7kppna/california-police-used-military-surveillance-tech-at-grad-student-strike) against strikers at UCSC as well as paying over 300k per day (by UCSC Chancellor Larives’s own estimates) on police presence at the picket line, busing them in from other UC campuses and neighboring counties. In the end, over 50 graduate students at Santa Cruz were fired from their TA positions. After a long fight, [all UCSC strikers were given their jobs back](https://www.vice.com/en_us/article/xg8mdn/uc-santa-cruz-reinstates-41-graduate-students-after-months-long-strike), but even then some still faced student conduct charges including up to 2 years suspension. Eventually those were also dropped, but it took more than a year of grads fighting UC and eventually a 3rd party arbitrator had to be brought in. 
    
- The best places to learn more are on Instagram @ucsb4cola and @payusmoreucsc, on Facebook @UCSB4COLA, on UC Santa Cruz’s [website](https://payusmoreucsc.com/wildcat-2020/), on [Strike University](http://strikeu.org)’s website (a source for strike-supportive alternative and radical education created by grads across the UC; networking with other grad students in the UC and wider CA college systems), and in [these](https://solidarity-us.org/atc/206/ucsc-wildcat-strike/) [articles](https://www.viewpointmag.com/2020/05/27/recording-the-complexity-of-struggle-an-interview-with-the-cola-agitation-committee/?fbclid=IwAR1oPiezuUfe-MSkOoiSLt7eVyQUFV0mkjmUC6LcMudYZDN21WaFokT_OLE).
    

- ### Cohort
    

- Your cohort is the group of students that entered the program at the same time as you. It’s important to get to know the people in your cohort and establish relationships, as y’all will likely be going through the same things together (e.g., taking the same courses, reaching milestones together, etc.). You don’t have to be best friends with everyone, but you should have a solid professional relationship with them at least.
    

- ### Classes
    

- Undergrad classes in the Soc department are very large. All of these classes have a lecture component, and some lower division undergrad classes (like Soc1 and the methods classes) also have sections.
    

- #### Lecture 
    

- Generally given by the instructor of record, this is more like a presentation that goes over key points and ideas, usually all the students in the class are in one large room.
    

- #### Section 
    

- Because lectures are usually too large for discussion, large classes also have additional sections. Each student is in one section (usually around 20-30 students) that meets in addition to the lecture. Generally, TAs lead sections and each TA has a few sections per week. In large classes like Soc 1, there is a large team of TAs and lots of sections per week.
    

- #### Graduate Seminar 
    

- Generally the class structure of what graduate students take is that of a small 12 person max seminar style. The professor may lecture for a bit, but usually the class is student-driven and discussion-based. For more information about course structure and what is expected from you in seminars see “Keys to Success in Grad School: What is expected from you.” 
    

- ### Faculty 
    

- #### Lecturers / non-senate faculty / adjunct professors 
    

- These faculty teach many undergraduate courses, have PhDs, and do research; however, they are not on the tenure track and thus have much less job security and stability and are paid considerably less. UC Lecturers are unionized with [UC-AFT](https://ucaft.org/). 
    

- #### Tenure 
    

- Professors go through an extensive review process to determine if they will be promoted to associate professor about 7-10 years after receiving their PhD. They are evaluated on their academic contributions (publications), their service in the department and the profession, and their teaching. If they are granted tenure, a professor cannot be fired, except under extraordinary circumstances. This assurance is intended to protect academic freedom -- a faculty member could not be let go just because they are researching an unpopular or controversial subject. Generally if someone does not receive tenure, they leave the institution. Read more [here](https://www.aaup.org/issues/tenure) and [here](https://www.insidehighered.com/advice/2009/03/27/what-i-wish-id-known-about-tenure), or do a google search because this is a complicated subject and varies from institution to institution. 
    
- A tenure-track position means that this professor is working toward receiving tenure.
    

- #### Academic Ladder 
    

- The [promotion pathway](https://bmap.berkeley.edu/sites/default/files/paths_of_progression_2016.pdf) for tenure-track positions; differs between institutions but generally lays out the promotions (“steps”) one can achieve, their order, and when professors are reviewed and can be next promoted. 
    

- #### Teaching Professor
    

- Tenure-track position that focuses on teaching rather than research (i.e., tenure requirements are related to teaching accomplishments rather than research requirements). Follow the same steps as research-focused professorships: Assistant, Associate, and Full. 
    

- #### Assistant Professor
    

- Entry level tenure-track professor position, on tenure-track but has not yet gone up for tenure. 
    

- #### Associate Professor 
    

- Mid level tenure-track professor position, has received tenure but has not yet been promoted to full professor. 
    

- #### Full Professor 
    

- Highest level tenure-track professor position besides “above scale” (even higher, extra) distinctions and raises.
    

- ### Academic journals 
    

- These publications are composed of articles, sometimes organized on a specific theme for a special issue. New issues are published at various frequencies depending on the journal. Standard journals publish articles about original research and/or theory. Review journals (such as [Annual Review of Sociology,](https://www.annualreviews.org/journal/soc) [Current Sociology](https://journals.sagepub.com/home/csi)) are more like long literature reviews that summarize the important research on a topic, so they are a great place to start if you are trying to learn about or find sources/studies on a topic! You can access various sociological journals through logging in at the [UCSB library website.](https://www.library.ucsb.edu) 
    
- #### Submit 
    

- The first stage of article publication is submission. After researching many journals to figure out where your article may best fit, you submit the article for review. Before submitting you should look at the journal's guidelines (e.g. word count/page limit, citation style, etc.).  
    

- #### Peer Review 
    

- Once your article is submitted, the journal editor will assign your article to 2-3 other scholars (usually with similar research interests as your article) to assess, critique, and provide feedback for your article. They ultimately give a recommendation to the editor if your article should be published. The editor will then decide to either accept your article for publication, reject the article, or will give you a revise and resubmit (R&R). No matter their decision, you will receive the feedback from each of the reviewers of your article. 
    

- #### R&R (revise & resubmit)
    

- If you receive an R&R, the editor believes that your article has potential to be published, but it still needs work. In this case, you will read the feedback from the reviewers, respond to each suggestion/critique, and revise your submission accordingly to make it better. You don’t have to change everything that the reviewers suggest (sometimes you won’t agree or even one reviewer’s suggestion will contradict another one), but if you don’t, you should have a reason why that is explained in your response to the reviewers. 
    

- #### “Top” sociology journals 
    

- Top is in quotes here because it is relative ([latest rankings](https://ooir.org/journals.php?category=sociology&metric=jif), see also this older page on [reputational rankings](https://scatter.wordpress.com/2020/01/29/devilish-details-a-reputational-ranking-of-generalist-and-specialist-sociology-journals/)). These journals are considered to be the most selective, most respected, and have the highest “impact factor” (measurement used to assess how often articles from that journal are cited in other publications), but they also favor more “old school” sociology methods and topics -- highly quantitative, macro approaches, “traditional” subjects. Nonetheless, if you publish in one of these journals, it’s a big deal.
    

- ASR (American Sociological Review) - most highly cited; run by ASA; more democratic review process
    
- AJS (American Journal of Sociology) - first sociology journal in the US; notoriety/popularity declining a bit; run by University of Chicago; long turnaround time between submission, review, and publication
    
- Social Forces - run by University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill; shorter word count
    
- Social Problems - run by The Society for the Study of Social Problems, higher word count than Social Forces
    

- ### Committee
    

- By your second year, you will form a committee of 3+ faculty members who will serve as your mentors and research supervisors. They ultimately are the ones who listen when you defend your master’s thesis, read your comps, defend your dissertation, and ultimately award you your PhD. You select a committee for your MA and can continue with the same committee or switch some or all of your committee members for your PhD. See [here](http://www.graddiv.ucsb.edu/academic/committees) for more info, requirements, and UCSB policies. 
    
- #### Chair 
    

- Heads your committee and serves as your lead mentor. You can also have two “co-chairs.”
    

- ### Professional development 
    

- Through your classes, you learn academic and scholastic knowledge; through professional development, you learn how to be a scholar. 
    
- #### Colloquium 
    

- Department events where a sociologist (usually from another institution) gives a research presentation, followed by Q&A. These are a great place to learn about what type of research is being done in sociology, network with sociologists outside of UCSB, and learn how sociologists talk about and present sociological knowledge. Generally we have colloquium over lunch hour once a month to once a week, depending on the colloquium committee and other events happening in the department. You should approach colloqia as if they are required events. Faculty and staff notice which students regularly attend, and they seem to evaluate grad student “engagement” primarily based on colloquium and job talk attendance.
    

- #### Job talk
    

- When the department is looking to hire a new professor, they will invite a few different applicants to visit the department, meet the students and faculty, and give a job talk, followed by Q&A. This presentation is basically like a colloquium in that the applicant will present the research they have conducted (generally their dissertation research if they are coming straight from graduate school) and where they plan to go from there. Job talks and other hiring events are great places to learn about what type of research is being done in sociology, to meet potential mentors (if they get hired, but you can also keep in touch with them even if they aren’t!), and learn what makes a successful job talk -- you’ll probably be giving one someday if you plan to go into academia. You should approach job talks as if they are required events. Faculty and staff notice which students regularly attend, and they seem to evaluate grad student “engagement” primarily based on colloquium and job talk attendance.
    

- ### Service
    

- Service can be either to the department/institution or to the field -- these are things that you do that do not directly relate to your scholarship but support and further your institution or the field of sociology in general.
    

- Service to the department/institution examples: serving on department committees, serving on student’s MA/PhD committee, holding a position in the department (chair, grad program director, etc), 
    
- Service to the field examples: serving as an editor of an academic journal, serving on a sociology association committee (holding a position in ASA/an ASA section), serving as a reviewer for journals and books presses
    

- While these are mostly obligations that come in to play when you become a professor, and are considered during tenure and review, having some service on your resumé is important as a graduate student going on the job market too
    

- Grad student service examples: serving on department committees, serving on SGSA, mentoring undergraduates in the Honors program, serving as graduate representatives in regional or national associations.

- ### First year
    

- First Year Mentor -- every new graduate student is assigned a first year mentor. While this mentor may be someone that you ask to serve on your committee, there is no pressure or expectation on you to ask them. Instead, this person is meant to assist you in the transition into graduate school and be a resource to help succeed. For the purposes of this guide, you can think of this first year mentor as the person who will help you during your first year whenever we mention something your chair can help you with. Additionally, this person can help you develop your research ideas/ interests and recommend committee members that suit those interests. Just like those serving on your committees, different professors will have different advising styles. If you know that you’re a person who needs monthly check-in meetings or if you know there’s something else you need to succeed, ask for it! Often it’s up to you to reach out and set up meetings, etc so get into the habit now!
    
- Mostly required courses; but this will be the year you have the most time for classes. Be sure to check your course selections with Monica and/or Sarah. 
    
- In general, first years take a 2- or 3-quarter sequence of theory courses, a 2-quarter sequence of quantitative methods courses, and a professional development seminar -- see your [cohort's handbook](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1euAoSnTFDCy-9MzmPNPaCYZB5x5z81DJ6jaSemKPExk/edit#heading=h.p59u6fl3gh5p) for details. 
    

- As of 2022, many course requirements for the M.A. have changed. So as always refer to your cohort’s handbook! Monica Gascon will hand out a handbook with specific requirements during your orientation!
    

- You’ll likely pick 1-2 courses per quarter in addition to your required courses. Many grads find it helpful to start with methods courses during your first year so that you have the tools and knowledge to begin data collection for your master’s thesis during the summer after your first year. 
    
- #### Fall
    

- Meet with potential committee members, join topic-specific associations, explore areas you might be interested in.
    
- Start to consider what you want your project to look like, including topic, research question(s), and methods. Salsa Dancing for the Social Sciences is a great resource. For qualitative researchers, Field Methods A (SOC 211A) often helps think through the project.
    
- If applying for fellowships, make sure that you communicate with Monica early on because often there is paperwork that she also has to do. Ideally, also meet with the UCSB Office of Research prior to applying to outside fellowships, but if you can’t connect with them, still apply to whatever it is.
    

- #### Winter
    

- Continue meeting with potential committee members and get to know faculty better, continue thinking about your Masters project and run some ideas past peers and faculty.
    

- #### Spring
    

- If you will be collecting primary data from humans, aim to submit your [IRB](https://www.research.ucsb.edu/human-subjects/about) package for the MA project by the middle of the spring term, so that you have approval in time to collect data over the summer.
    

- #### Summer
    

- Note: depending on your funding package and personal finances, you might need to TA over the summer. Take this into consideration when creating your timeline.
    
- Ideally, you will find a dataset, collect data, or otherwise start working toward your master’s thesis over the summer. 
    
- Consider whether you’ll need to travel either for your research or personal reasons and plan how to fund that in advance.
    
- Remember to take it easy. You just made it through your first year woo! Plan slowly. Your first summer and year do not have many other expectations other than preparing and organizing yourself for the next 5 years. If you have an M.A. idea, great! If not, don’t stress, this is the time to read, inquire, and discover your own academic research interests. 
    

- ### Second year
    

- Many students find it works best to only take 1-2 classes per quarter if you are also TAing. Remember that you will have (at least) 3 responsibilities in that case: TAing, course work, and your thesis work
    
- This is the year that you should really focus on your MA, so that you can finish it by the end of 2nd year/before the start of the 3rd year. If it takes a little longer than that, it isn’t the end of the world – but you should plan to have it done by then.
    
- Make sure you’ve taken the classes necessary for the MA -- check with Sarah and/or Monica.
    

- ### Third year
    

- Aim to defend your MA by fall.
    
- Try to finish comps this year.
    

- ### Fourth year
    

- In order to remain in good academic standing, you need to complete your comps and defend your dissertation prospectus by the end of the fourth year. 
    

- ### Fifth year and beyond
    

- Tentatively plan to finish dissertation during your 5th year.
    
- If planning to go into academia, tentatively plan to go on the job market in the fall (maybe not doing a wide application process, but instead strategically choosing a few places and applying to them). When you actually defend your dissertation will depend on how the job market pans out for you. If you haven’t found something that fits, you’ll stay another year and try again. At this stage, things don’t always follow a linear trajectory.

- A therapist
    
- A hobby
    
- Non-major friends - see the section on Social Life to learn about campus-wide associations where you can meet other grads
    
- Non-friend collaborators
    
- A creative outlet
    
- A political outlet
    

- UCSB may have a progressive sheen but academia is a deeply conservative institution with very few protective systems for redress (the union being one of them) and is bound to reproduce systems of oppression. It's important that folks have safe spaces to vent, cope, get collective support, and organize for change. The healthiest way to be in an institution is also to be organizing against it!
    

- Several people (either formal mentors or professional connections) who can do some of the following:
    

- Give you advice about the job market, especially if you’re thinking about teaching in a Sociology-adajecent field like Feminist Studies, Black Studies, etc. (a recently tenured or non-tenured professor is a good source for this, especially in those departments) [And give you advice on non-academic jobs and be honest with you about how bad the current academic job market is]
    
- Give you advice on being a human in academia (grad students who are further along in the program are helpful for this)

- Provide a space to explore a topic you want to study but isn’t offered in depth

This can be true despite everything your mentors do to make themselves approachable and non-threatening. Sometimes being able to run things by someone who doesn’t have that authority can help give you the confidence to push through and grow more comfortable working with your committee.
    
- An important note however is that if you have a mentor who is more than someone you occasionally talk to (i.e. is given substantial feedback on things you have written or other substantive advice) then you should seriously consider asking them to be on your committee as they are already doing a lot of work for you.
    
- Other grad students can be a great resource!
    

- ## What is expected of you 
    

- ### General
    

- In general, you are expected to initiate pretty much everything related to your education and training. By the time someone comes and asks if you have done xyz it is because those things should have been finished already, and they are hoping you just forgot to sign the paperwork. This will vary greatly depending on interpersonal relationships, but the general assumption is that (1) If you need help, you will ask for it, (2) you will be following up with people rather than them following up with you, (3) you will stay on top of all of the paperwork and other logistical aspects of your advancement, and (4) you know what you are doing.
    
- That last one, (4), will frequently feel like a lie. It isn’t. Imposter syndrome is very real and very common. You got this far and you do know what you are doing. The trick is that you could also be doing it better which is why you are here in the first place. Both of these are true at the same time even though they sound kinda contradictory. You can find more information and resources about imposter syndrome [here](https://www.apa.org/gradpsych/2013/11/fraud) and [here](https://www.chronicle.com/article/impostor-syndrome-is-definitely-a-thing/).
    

- ### Classes
    

- Classes won’t teach you in the same way they did in undergrad. Professors design their own pedagogy and so each class is different, but in our experience, most professors approach grad seminars as a group discussion that they moderate rather than a lecture that they give. This means that the majority of teaching actually occurs outside of class time. The professors use their expertise to guide you to the most important works and trust that you are able to read them and understand why they are important. Class then is a time for students to meet and discuss the things that they have ostensibly taught themselves by reading. You will get more out of these discussions  when you do the reading (more on this below), engage in the readings, ask critical questions, and listen to your peers. 
    
- You will get the most out of classes when you learn to balance stepping up and sitting back. If you find it easy to step up and be active in discussions then continue to talk, but pay close attention to ensure other students are talking just as much as you are. If it is easy for you to sit back and let others lead the discussion then it is very important that you grow more comfortable talking and taking a more active role, particularly in groups. Being able to be vocal about what you need to be taught while being tactful and avoiding dominating conversations will help you in all stages of grad school. 
    

- A helpful strategy is to come up with 2-3 questions that critically engage with the readings and write them up. Then you already have some contributions to make in class and don’t have to stress about finding something to bring up.
    

- You simply will not be able to do an in-depth reading of everything that is assigned in your classes--sometimes you won’t even have time to skim every article. If you try to read every page, you will never do anything else but that during the quarter. Here are some suggestions:
    

- First of all, learn how to skim!!!
    
- For every class, it’s smart to coordinate reading with other students (In our experience groups of 2-3 work best)).The idea is that you divide the reading into equal parts and each member reads a different part in-depth and skim the others. As a group you will have read all of the material in-depth and as individuals you will have each at least skimmed everything. 
    

- There are several ways of doing this, but most are some variation on the following. Each person commits to doing a set of the assigned readings, taking notes (using a mutually-agreed-upon template) and sharing with the rest of the exchange 1-2 days before class. This is a way to be prepared for class without trying to do 200-400 pages of reading per class per week (which is impossible). 
    

- ### Research 
    

- The various milestones (MA, Comp, etc) have historically been loosely structured, which has both benefits and downsides. Department leadership has been working to make expectations more concrete for newer cohorts, so make sure to check your [cohort's handbook](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1euAoSnTFDCy-9MzmPNPaCYZB5x5z81DJ6jaSemKPExk/edit#heading=h.p59u6fl3gh5p). As with everything, these milestones vary depending on your committee. But no matter your committee or cohort year, you will have to make a lot of your own decisions about what these milestones look like for you. And this is part of the point! You have to demonstrate the ability to make decisions on how to accomplish the task and to defend those decisions. In other words, the fact that you will find yourself making important decisions regarding your project (even/especially if you feel like committee members should be the ones deciding) is a feature, not a bug. A first step to doing this is to create a five to six year plan, as explained in other sections and in the book “The Professor is In.”
    
- Also, introduce yourself to and get comfortable asking questions of more advanced students!
    
- Start brainstorming and planning potential research projects on day 1. As soon as you have some ideas, talk them over with your professors and advisors. Likely, no one will check in on you during your first year to see how this is going unless you reach out first. They aren’t going to hold your hand through the process either. If you aren’t careful, you could easily finish your first year after focusing on classes and then realize that you have no plan for your MA research, which is supposed to be complete by the end of the next year! 
    
- For every class and assignments, think about how you can use it to further your research project. Can you find quantitative data sources about your topic to explore in the Quant Methods series? Can you use a paper assignment to write a literature review or grant/fellowship proposal? Professors are often flexible about final assignments and often like it when you make it useful to you. 
    
- The best way to learn how to do research is by doing it! You likely won’t feel like you’re prepared to start collecting and analyzing data, but you probably never will until you get real hands-on experience. 
    
- #### MA Thesis
    

- Format of this varies widely depending on your committee and cohort year, as the expectations have been made more explicit over the years. Regardless, plan to have your first publication come from this thesis. Most faculty believe that your MA thesis should not be any more than one article.
    
- It’s strategic, but not necessary, to consider your MA thesis 
    

- A dry run or a smaller version of your dissertation. 
    
- The first good draft of your first journal article
    

- Ask your chair to refer you to other students they have chaired MA thesis committees for. Ask those students about the processes. 
    
- You can look up sample MA theses here (but understand that the grad program has changed over the years, so your MA thesis might look different): [https://www.proquest.com/pqdt/results/FB4FE52228CF4116PQ/1?accountid=14522](https://www.proquest.com/pqdt/results/FB4FE52228CF4116PQ/1?accountid=14522)
    

- #### Qualifying Exams/“Comps”
    

- Qualifying exams are the exams we take to qualify for PhD candidacy. When you pass them you will “advance to candidacy” or “go ABD” (all but dissertation). These exams vary program to program. In our department, there are 2 qualifying exams that you complete after you have defended your MA. Plan to start working on this about a month after you defend your MA (you’ll need a break) and expect it to take 3-6 months. You complete these comps to demonstrate that you have mastered a specific set of subfields within Sociology. You get to choose the subfields, with the approval of your committee.
    
- New Comp Exam Structure - cohorts 22/23 and later.
    

- Select between two options
    

1. Field essays in response to questions
    
2. Special Fields paper
    

- You must also take a course in an exam area of your choosing. 
    
- Refer to your [cohort's handbook](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1euAoSnTFDCy-9MzmPNPaCYZB5x5z81DJ6jaSemKPExk/edit#heading=h.p59u6fl3gh5p) for more information.
    

- Old comp exam structure - cohorts 20/21 and earlier.
    

- First comp is a set of papers from 3 seminars you’ve taken on the same sub-field (e.g. race, ethnicity, nation (REN) or gender). This is typically the easier of the two papers because you will likely have taken these classes even if this weren’t a requirement. 
    
- Second comp is a new paper you write that focuses on a more specific or nuanced topic that relates to your research interests, either generally or regarding a specific project. . Because it would be impossible to write a paper that truly covers everything, part of this test is that it is up to you to determine what is important enough about your topic to include in this paper and what isn’t. This paper is reviewed by 2 “readers.” Your chair will (almost certainly) be one of these readers and both readers are meant to be experts on your chosen topic. The requirements for this paper are entirely up to your readers (typically your chair). Some common iterations are:
    

- You write a survey paper that covers your subject in a comprehensive manner. Think of this option as you writing a chapter in a textbook for an undergrad course that explains everything of importance about your topic. 
    
- You are establishing the theoretical framework and context of a specific research project (i.e. your dissertation).
    
- You are given a set of specific questions about your subject to answer.
    

- Monica has a binder full of example papers you can read. These are most helpful if they were written for your committee members, so again, ask people who share some of your committee members for samples, especially if Monica doesn’t have any that help you. 
    

- #### Dissertation
    

- The dissertation is the big scary thing that you write to finish the  PhD. Joking aside, your dissertation is the single most important thing you will write in grad school. Somewhere between starting grad school and defending your dissertation you are meant to effectively switch places with your committee. In other words, by the time you defend your dissertation, you will have more knowledge and expertise on your subject than your committee. This loops back to how your committee isn’t primarily there to teach you, but to train you.
    
- Despite its importance, writing a dissertation is several years in the future. While you should begin thinking about a dissertation so that you can be strategic with your choices, it is more important that you be general with your plans so that you maintain flexibility and give yourself room to adapt as you become a more experienced scholar. 
    
- Like everything else in grad school, there is a great deal of variation in how to write a dissertation. The two most common variations are:
    

- A set of 3-4 interrelated articles 
    
- A first draft of a book
    

- ### Professionalization
    

- #### What if I don’t want to go into academia?
    

- It is completely and perfectly acceptable not to want to be a professor, but you must have something in mind regarding your career path. Also, your mentors may not be able to help you very much because they all became professors. 
    
- If you think you might want to go the non-academic route, get to know Lana Smith-Hale in the Graduate Career Services Department. She will have resources to help with this.
    
- Check out panels and workshops about jobs outside of academia at conferences. ASA and SWS typically have workshops about building resumes for outside academia jobs.
    
- [Beyond Academia](https://beyondacademia.ucsb.edu/) is a program at the UCs for PhD students looking for jobs outside of academia. UCSB Beyond Academia hosts an annual conference in the spring at UCSB. 
    

- #### Colloquia
    

- Right now, these are Mondays 12-1:30 in SSMS 3317 (sometimes these are hybrid via zoom), but that can change, so keep an eye on your email and the department website calendar.
    
- Out of sight out of mind- The more faculty and staff see you the better you will do. If you can go to something then you should. If you can’t go to something you should try and find a way to go anyway. Simply being present is great, being able to ask a question is even better. Many faculty very much notice which grads attend department events and which don’t. It’s better to be one of the ones that does! This also goes for job talks (when candidates the department is thinking about hiring as professors come to visit campus and give a formal talk to the full department about their research).
    
- Most of the things you learn in grad school you learn through exposure and experience rather than someone actually teaching you. This means that the more exposure and experience you can get the better you will do. It also means that in the long run, attending the hour colloquium that is on something you don’t care about at all will be more valuable than an hour of writing. Both are important, but only attending the colloquium will give you exposure to how scholars present themselves and their research. 
    

- #### External conferences
    

- You should consider going to at least one a year. Conferences are a great place where you can present your research, network with other scholars and graduate students, and attend workshops/events geared toward professional development. Usually, neither the department nor GSA will provide funding to go to one unless you are presenting, so save money in your budget for this, arrange to share AirBnBs and travel with friends, and choose economically. Additionally some conferences offer funding for graduate students - it is important to do this research when choosing which conference to attend. The Sociology Department Calendar on Google Calendar includes major dates and deadlines for these conferences. 
    
- Below are the major conferences you should be aware of and include in your 5-6 year plan:
    

- [American Sociological Association (ASA) Annual conference:](https://www.asanet.org/annual-meeting) this is our field’s biggest conference. It’s held every year and alternates between being on the East Coast and the West Coast. Economically it makes sense to wait to go on a year when it’s on the West Coast, so that you can drive there, but always take a look. They are usually around August.
    
- [Pacific Sociological Association](https://www.pacificsoc.org/) (PSA): this is kind of an off-shoot of the ASA that is regionally focused. A good starter conference because it’s a bit smaller and often drivable. They are usually around spring break, and alternate being located in California one year, then another West Coast state in the next year.
    
- [Sociologists for Women in Society](https://socwomen.org/) (SWS):  is a nonprofit professional feminist organization that centers the development of sociological feminist theory and scholarship. Another great starter conference, and known to be a “friendly” conference at which to present a paper -- especially for grad students because of their cost-share program for grads and the fact that a lot of the food is catered! They have two conferences a year - a winter conference, usually in January, and a summer conference, which overlaps with ASA. SWS typically has events/happy hours at PSA as well because there is [a West coast chapter](https://socwomen.org/sws-west/).
    

- ### TAing
    

- #### Being a TA serves several purposes 
    

- Paycheck: The vast majority of our funding comes in the form of TAships. Typically, you will start TAing your second year of grad school. The primary reason we TA is so that we can get paid. 
    
- Teaching Experience: TAing also gives us very valuable teaching experience. Check your [cohort's handbook](https://docs.google.com/document/d/1euAoSnTFDCy-9MzmPNPaCYZB5x5z81DJ6jaSemKPExk/edit#heading=h.p59u6fl3gh5p) for requirements about what types of classes you will need to TA.
    
- Training: Another benefit of TAing is that it actually provides a great deal of further training regarding the course topic. By teaching the material and grading assignments you will strengthen your own understanding and expertise.
    

- If you’re looking for TA experience outside of the department, [look here.](https://groups.google.com/a/sa.ucsb.edu/g/GraduateStudentTAOpportunites) 
    

- #### General expectations of TAs
    

- Communicate with the Instructor! The most important expectation is that there is clear and open communication between you as a TA and the instructor. This communication goes both ways. 
    

- ASE Checklist- This checklist is from our Union and it details the various tasks that you can be asked to do as a TA. Each quarter you should email the instructor so you can go over this checklist together so that the instructor can articulate what they need you to do for that class and for you to articulate what is reasonable. The majority of work as a TA is the same regardless of the class you are assigned, and so this is typically just the professor going over their course assignments with you and confirming that the amount of grading is doable. While this likely sounds very intimidating, the instructors are all very used to this process which helps to relieve pressure and help things run smoothly. 
    
- Meetings- There is a line in the ASE checklist regarding meeting with the instructor every week or so. Unless you are TAing for a course with multiple TAs (Soc 1 and Soc 108), the instructor probably won’t find this necessary. If you don’t have a standing meeting, it’s very common to have frequent informal meetings with your instructor before or after lecture or to check-in and share info via email. No matter the method, the important thing is that you establish a relationship with the instructor, so that you can open communication with them easily and quickly if / when problems or concerns arise. 
    

- Grade- The most labor intensive aspect of being a TA is grading. Most classes have 80-90 students and only a single TA. Keep this in mind when going over the course with the instructor. A 6 page paper isn’t a lot to grade until you have 90 of them. 
    

- Track your time- It is important that you have at least a rough idea of how long you spend working for 2 reasons. Use this [workload grid](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1JcyD744aV4RCGcDhPnJx_jV0srDaN-Wf2W5s5XS8zYc/copy) to track your hours -- seriously.
    

- We cannot be asked to work more than 8 hours per day, 20 hours per week, and/or 220 hours per quarter – it violates the work contract we, as a unionized collective of workers, have negotiated with the university. If you are having to work more than that, don’t hesitate to communicate with the instructor to reduce the workload. If that doesn’t work, reach out to the UCSB grad union reps -- see the section below “Your rights as a worker.”
    
- If you know how long it takes you to grade an average final paper or midterm then you will be better able to gauge just how much work is reasonable to expect of you. Most people, including professors, underestimate the amount of time it takes to grade assignments. In contrast, most TAs can invest too much time grading (more on this below).
    

- Office Hours- All TAs need to have 2 office hours a week. These can be a single 2-hour chunk or 2 1-hour chunks. It’s up to you if you’d like to hold them in person or on Zoom. If you want to have in-person office hours, you can use the TA offices in the Soc Department (ask Madhu in the Soc Dept office to assign you a key) or hold them anywhere else on campus you’d like. If you want to have some office hours in a public space (e.g. library, outside, etc.), at least one hour should be in a confidential space (e.g., an actual office, on Zoom) so that you can discuss sensitive topics like grades. Some TAs find it helpful to use sites like Calandy to have students sign up for specific office hour appointments – 15 minutes for each appointment is a good default because it allows you to meet with 8 students over 2 hours. Sadly very few students attend office hours, so if you don’t have them sign up ahead of time and instead sit there and let them come by as needed, it is wise to have other things that you can work on.
    
- Attend Lecture- Attending lecture is a fairly self explanatory expectation. Check with your instructor regarding what, if anything, they would like you to do during lecture (distribute handouts, help with group activities, etc) While some instructors are happy for you to sit in the back of the room and work on grading or your own work, others are not.
    
- Lead sections (if the class you’re TAing for has sections) - Check GOLD to see if the class you’re TAing for has sections. If it does, you’ll need to coordinate with any other TAs for that class to see which section you’ll be TAing for. Leading section usually means leading a review session of the previous week’s lectures, and offering students an opportunity to get their questions answered. A good section will split time between admin reminders, a group activity, and open time for students to ask questions about the material. If they have an assignment due soon (like a paper), you could also use the time to workshop their drafts. You should also plan to create and distribute a section syllabus. More information can be found on the GauchoSpace “course” called “SOC TA Training”
    
- Check out the [UCSB Office of Instructional Development](https://id.ucsb.edu/teaching/ta-training/ta-workshops) for their TA workshops and certificate programs. For example, if you complete all the [Pillars of TAing](https://id.ucsb.edu/teaching/teaching-resources/ta-programs/pillars-of-teaching-assistantship) workshops, you get a certificate to add to your CV that can help you stand out in comparison to other TAs (this is especially helpful if you want to teach courses in another department).
    

- #### Good things we want to do, but shouldn’t
    

- Full disclaimer: Many of us love teaching and helping people flourish, so we dislike this section. However, there is only so much any one person can do and only so much time.  
    
- Things you shouldn’t do because it’ll take up more time and emotional energy than you have:
    

- Fix all of the social problems that we see in the classroom--it is very disheartening to see differences in how students perform in ways that are clearly linked to privilege and to want to invest more of ourselves in helping those students. This is very good and noble, and it will also burn you out. Love your students and help them in every way you can, but remember that you have to take care of yourself too. 
    
- Give lots of feedback on every assignment--you should spend no more than 10 minutes grading a paper. Some recommend no more than 7 minutes or even as little as 5. Expect the first few papers to take you longer, but if you spend 30 minutes grading every paper and giving amazing feedback you will burnout and not have any time to do your own research. 
    

- The single exception to this is research methods (108). Enrollment for 108 is capped at 60 and there are 2 TAs who each have 30 students. The reason for this cap is precisely so that you can give detailed feedback on assignments. But you’ll still need to figure out how detailed that feedback can be.
    

- Things you shouldn’t do because you do not have the training to do them:
    

- Be a proxy therapist for your students -- You will be amazed (and possibly horrified) at what students will tell you. While it is expected that you are very kind and friendly with all of your students, you are not their friend. Support your students when they struggle and work with the instructor to be flexible, but your sphere of influence stops with your single course. The best thing you can do to support students is to help them find the resources and people who can do what you cannot do as their TA.

- ## Note on Activism
    

- Sociology is a science. The products of science and activism go very well together and this relationship draws many activists to academia. In other words, the knowledge that science produces helps inform activism and combat many social problems. Professor Victor Rios in our department is a great example of this, as is George Lipsitz. They are brilliant scholars whose work has had a huge impact, which is in part because their work is scientifically rigorous. It is very important to remember that science is primarily a method. It is very easy to approach a research project from the viewpoint of righting social wrongs in a way that (potentially) inadvertently invalidates your methodology. When this happens your committee will rightly correct you, but this can feel like they are critiquing your activism when they are trying to critique your science. Thankfully it is very possible to do both science and activism in a way that they strengthen each other and many people in the department are committed to doing exactly that.
    
- Some keywords that scholars use to reference this (keeping in mind that these are, by far, not all the same thing. Each with its own history, methods, and epistemic commitments, etc):
    

- Public sociology
    
- Scholar activism
    
- Engaged scholarship
    
- Praxis
    
- Critical scholarship
    
- Critical participatory action research
    

- In addition to many marginalized disciplines that are built off of and incorporate inherently political orientations and question normativity. To name a few:
    

- Ethnic studies
    
- Feminist studies
    
- Indigenous studies
    
- Black studies
    

- If you’re interested in reading more about how sociology and social justice can go hand-in-hand, here are some well-known articles on public sociology:
    

- “[For Public Sociology](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/000312240507000102)” Michael Burroway
    
- “[Toward a Field of Intersectional Studies: Theory, Applications, and Praxis](https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/10.1086/669608)” Sumi Cho and Kimberlé Crenshaw

- Don’t depend on caffeine

- Toddler troubleshooting method:
    

- Are you hungry?
    
- Are you thirsty?
    
- Have you taken a walk lately?
    
- Do you need a nap?
    
- If those fail, go home and try again tomorrow. Taking breaks when you need them really matters. If you burn out, it WILL BE HARD TO RECOVER.
    

- Grad school and your research will try to eat your life. If you would like to stay in a healthy space, do not let them. Remember grad school is not a lifestyle but work, so treat it like a job. 
    

- Set time aside where you don’t check school emails or do school work. The emails will fill your inbox relentlessly, and you will always have a new task, make time for yourself through that. There are not sociological emergencies – any email you received since the last time you checked (which is honestly probably earlier that day or yesterday) is surely not so urgent that you must check again at midnight instead of just going to bed.
    

- There is nothing wrong with your priorities. As long as you can make them work, great. Don’t let people tell you that something is unimportant.
    
- It’s not what you think. It’s that you think. (e.g. are you thinking in logical and consistent ways? Are you informed by emotional components without being driven by them?) The ‘what you think’ without being reflexive about your own methods and thought process means bias. Thankfully that same reflexivity is also an antidote to fear and insecurity.
    
- Take advantage of all the resources that UCSB has to offer, especially those specifically geared toward helping grads, like the [Graduate Student Resource Center](http://www.gradpost.ucsb.edu/). See the last section on resources for tons more suggestions!
    

- ### Tips for Classes
    

- Don’t try to do all of the reading in-depth. Hone your skimming skills. This can be hard to master, but is essential. Even after you finish course work, you’ll still have a ton of reading that you cannot possibly read every single word of.
    

- [EverydaySociology blog on reading articles](https://www.everydaysociologyblog.com/2021/09/how-to-read-a-sociological-journal-article-for-beginners.html)
    
- Prof Hannah Wohl is very knowledgeable about how to effectively skim – pay her a visit :)
    
- ### Text-to-audio software allows you to listen to readings!
    

- You can listen while going for a walk, commuting, cleaning, cooking, or doing simple activities like knitting. There are many programs out there, here’s one with free version: [Natural Readers](https://www.naturalreaders.com/index.html) - available through web-interface and/or app, can download audio to listen offline
    

- Work collaboratively with your peers. Create or join study groups with those in your class. 
    
- If you don’t understand a concept, theoretical framework, or Stata command, ask about it. If you don’t understand it, someone else probably doesn’t either! 
    
- In undergrad you learned about subjects or things. The marker of success was your ability to remember and present those facts in a way that demonstrated you understood them. In grad school, the point isn’t to learn existing knowledge about things, but rather to learn about the various fields of knowledge and techniques of creating new knowledge to advance those fields.
    
- One key difference between experts and novices is that novices organize their thoughts in terms of facts and formulas but experts organize their thoughts around big concepts and applications. This helps distinguish when and how certain facts are relevant and useful for whatever problem they are solving. For classes, this translates to learning the big core concepts in a deep manner, rather than trying to understand everything in detail that is discussed. See [this article](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1o1gRYNsNibzZ9dWMXWHNnHWes9szxiVo/view) for more information. 
    

- ### Tips for TAing
    

- Feedback number method:
    

- Write the common feedback you give on student assignments on a separate handout and assign each comment a number (or number and sub-number if there are common variations). Then when grading, instead of writing the entire comment 50+ times you can just write the number. When you give the assignments back to the students you can also give the comment handout (either print copies or gauchospace depending on instructor preference). This both saves you a ton of time grading, but it also makes your feedback a bit more confidential since comments you make are hidden (e.g. if there is a student who did poorly it doesn’t say “This needs a lot of work” on the front of their assignment)
    

- Don’t grade when you are hungry, angry, or sleepy - when you do grade under these conditions you may grade harder than what you normally would. 
    
- Build in rewards and breaks into grading - It is often helpful to group assignments into batches and to set a break and reward when you get through a batch. For example, if you have 80 papers to grade you might break those into groups of 5. You can set the goal to grade 20 papers a day, which is 4 chunks, and after you finish a chunk you take a break to play a game or watch silly youtube videos. 
    
- Grade blindly when possible - grading assignments without know which student you are grading until you are done helps you and your students for at least 2 reasons:
    

- It helps to avoid unconsciously grading some students more or less favorably due to any unconscious bias we may have.
    
- It helps us identify those same biases when they slip through. Once we see those biases in ourselves we can correct them, but we can’t correct them until we see them. 
    

- Grade ceilings- Set maximum penalties for certain errors
    

- There are a number of things that will influence a student’s grade that are not things that the course is designed to teach them. Grammar and writing are the most common. By setting a grade ceiling for anticipated errors like this (e.g. A student can’t lose more than 2% for writing errors) you can help prevent yourself from focusing on things that the class isn’t designed to teach even though they might otherwise distract you from the more important aspects of the student’s work.
    

- ### Tips for Research
    

- BACK UP YOUR WORK!! 
    

- Use your [Box](https://www.box.com/home) (i.e. unlimited storage space, available through UCSB), [Dropbox](https://www.dropbox.com/business/landing-t61fl?_tk=paid_sem_goog_biz_b&_camp=142947702&_kw=dropbox%7Ce&_ad=389661549544%7C%7Cc&gclid=EAIaIQobChMInNKg1P6g6wIVAhLnCh3jtQgOEAAYASAAEgLGj_D_BwE) (not available through UCSB, but available for free, limited storage space) and/or UCSB Google Drive. IN ADDITION, have a physical backup of all of your things on a flash drive/external hard drive. A flash drive in your work bag / on your keychain can also be handy, especially if you like to work on computers in the library, have to print teaching materials on the soc dept printer, etc.
    

- The [library](https://www.library.ucsb.edu/research) is an amazing resource and librarians are very skilled at helping you find the research you are looking for.
    
- Rubber duck method: Have a designated inanimate object for when you are stuck. Explain step-by-step both what you are doing and why you are doing it to that object out loud. Speaking out loud and explaining what you are doing takes advantage of how we better understand things when we teach them to others. Plus you don’t have to wonder whether the rubber duck is judging you. Example 1, if you are stuck because your code for statistics isn’t working explain what each individual line of code does to the rubber duck. Example 2, if you are coding qualitative data and are getting lost and flustered with all of your codes then explain to the rubber duck what each of your codes are and how you know to apply each code. 
    
- Be able to give a one sentence summary of what you are working on. Second, have an elevator pitch. If you are in an elevator for 30 seconds with your dream mentor and/or collaborator, what do you tell them? (assume field specific knowledge, avoid jargon, don’t condescend and don’t overwhelm). You will have to explain what you’re interested in/studying often, it’s good to have it on-hand - especially for when you attend conferences. 
    
- Your PhD is for training purposes, meaning you are here to learn how to do things, so focus on the skills you want to acquire and tie them to interests. Do not focus on shiny new ideas and connect them to techniques you already know. Focus on reexamining ideas you already care about using new methods and skills.
    
- When your committee gives you critical feedback it means they still think you can succeed because if they didn’t think you could do it they wouldn’t let you try.
    
- Be confident in both yourself and your ideas, but always be open to the fact that your concepts can be disproven by new data. Try to disprove your own theories (falsifiability). If you cannot test your theory it isn’t a theory (testability). Your ideas aren’t supposed to be good, they’re supposed to be defensible -- what you do is far less important than how you do it. 
    

- ### Tips for Navigating the Academy/Department
    

- Make friends with the staff -- they work so hard to help you!
    
- Check your email at least once every weekday 
    

- So many important notifications and useful opportunities come through email. It can be overwhelming, but you’ll regret it if you miss that $500 grant or the deadline to turn in your hiring paperwork (there’ll most likely be a delay in your paycheck), not to mention that those trying to help you (staff, faculty, etc) will get frustrated if you keep missing communications. 
    

- There is a difference between removing your identity and presenting a professional face. Make a professional face that includes your personality that also affords people a chance to take you seriously (especially if they otherwise wouldn’t). Becoming a member of a subculture (i.e. academia) and developing cultural competence by learning its associated language and skills does not require assimilating to a hegemonic group and losing yourself and passion. Professionalization has a lot to do with class and white-collar work, and performing a certain middle to upper-class culture. It is not about how other people see you (as serious or not) but seeing yourself as responsible, capable, powerful, and committed, not regardless but because of your identity.
    
- Get to know more advanced students (and alumni) whenever possible. It’s totally socially acceptable to reach out cold to them, and share who you are and ask to take them out for coffee to hear about their work/job/time in the department/advice. This can save a LOT of time spent floundering around on your own!
    

- ### Tips for conferences
    

- It's never too early to start thinking about conferences! If you can swing it financially (there are some funding resources through the department and through GSA), they are great professional development and networking opportunities.
    
- To keep costs down:
    

- Have roommates to lower the cost of boarding 
    
- Staying at the conference hotel is almost never required, and it’s often WAY more expensive than a lot of other housing in most cities -- it’s also on site and therefore can maximize your time, though, so it’s a tradeoff -- whatever you decide, at least explore other options like hostels, Air BnB, etc.
    
- Figure out when and which receptions will be serving free food/drinks; most conferences will either have a student reception or a section reception where there will be a free food and/or a drink
    
- Some conferences, mainly ASA, will have a "student book giveaway" on the last day of the conference.
    
- Figure out and apply for funding from the conferences themselves
    

- At the conference:
    

- Network, network, network! Go to receptions and meet people -- you never know who you'll meet. Go to the talk of someone whose research you’re interested in -- this will give you an excuse to go up to them later or send them an email later introducing yourself.
    
- It's a marathon and not a sprint. Don't try to go to all of the sessions or you'll burn out or get conference fatigue (especially conferences like ASA). Some people find 2-3 sessions/workshops per day a good amount, but obviously this will depend on you.
    
- Remember to take breaks and try squeezing in a nap if you can.
    
- Bring a reusable water bottle and/or coffee tumbler. Take small snacks with you, especially if the sessions you plan on attending are back-to-back.
    
- If you are wanting to go to two sessions but they happen at the same time, see if your friends are going to it and are willing to take notes you can share. You could email the people who are presenting to see if they have a publication or recent draft of the work they are presenting on.
    
- In order to remember what is being discussed in the session, take notes or live-tweet the sessions.
    
- If you are looking to do service work, check out open committee meetings. Going to open committee meetings or business meetings are not required; however they are useful to see how organizations are run and planned.
    
- Pay particular attention to events that are catered toward students: you can network with other students (who will likely later be your colleagues in academia)
    

- ### Tips for Burnout
    

- Research is inherently creative, you need to NOT THINK about your research long enough for the back of your brain to figure it out. Insight and clarity come from the part of your brain that isn’t consciously thinking about.
    
- Time off is just as, if not more, important than work. Unplug regularly. Preventing burnout is easier than recovering from it. Preventative measures never feel like they do anything because their entire point is that you don’t see an effect so resist the urge to devalue time off because it feels unproductive. 
    
- Rest, don’t just be not working.
    

- Watching Netflix is not the same thing as taking a break because there is a difference between “not working” and “taking a break.” Just because you have stopped working doesn’t mean you are resting and recovering. Make sure to rest.
