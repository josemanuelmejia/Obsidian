Abstract
The metabolic structural processes of the capitalist world-economy have produced historical antisystemic resistances with world-scale effects over capital's extended reproduction. In this article, we explain the world-scale effects of antimining struggles in Los Andes over the capital reproduction circuit of imperialist bank state capitalists trusts in competition under the breakout conditions of Sino-American shared imperialism from three case studies: i) the cholo-peasantry antimining struggles in Cajamarca (Peru) during the 2004 and 2011-2012 against the Newmont Gold Mining Corporation headed in the U.S., ii) the indigenous-peasantry mining struggles in Apurimac (Peru) during 2015 and 2019 against the MMG of China Minmetals Corporation (CMC), and iii) the indigenous-cholo peasantry antimining struggles in Cotopaxi (Ecuador) during 20232024 against the Atico Mining Corporation headed in Canada. The theory-building case studies conducted have been nurtured by fieldwork, archive research, and protest event data analysis to show the consistency of the hypothesis proposed.
Keywords: Peru; Ecuador; China; United States of America; Canada; Antimining struggles; Antisystemic movements; Capital reproduction; Gold; Shared imperialism.

2.	Antisystemic resistance of historical challengers: the antimining movement of the indigenous-cholo Andean peasantry
The formation of the class structure of the modern world-system is founded on the racialized international division of labor produced by colonization as a policy of expansion of merchant capital. Around ten million “indigenous" survived the genocide planned in Los Andes of an estimated total of sixty five million exterminated over the continental “America", being sublimated as inferior beings and reduced to mere labor force by the colonization method of primitive accumulation (Marx [1886]1976:873- 875, 914; Mariategui, January 16, 1929:78; Quijano and Wallerstein 1992; Quijano [1999], 2007, 169-170; Mignolo 2011, 183-84).
The world no longer fit into the consciousness of the survivors. But in their collective consciousness a myth survived. Or a myth makes sense in our consciousness, it is the myth of the new indigenous, of the Inkarri (the Inka return), of the Yawar-Inti (Sun of Blood) and the resurgence of the “indigenous" world by a revolutionary vendetta. In opposition to Arboleda's (2020) interpretation of the formation of indigenous “identity politics"5, I argue that the revolutionary subjectivity that conjures up indigenous socialism is a historical formation in movement, a block of living historical text clashing, developing the peasantry as an antagonist class in formal subsumption since the sixteenth century.
For both Arboleda (2020) and Botfvar Echevarna, the indigenous peoples could not understand the antagonism between them and the Spaniards. Their cosmology, they believe, did not allow the indigenous to realize that they were being killed by an external collective antagonistic to their lives or “collective self”. If someone follows that argument, by implication this indigenous “self-defeating cultural essentialisms” would only change with the later

2.1.	The Historical Resistance of the Cholo-Indigenous Peasant Struggles Against Imperialist Mining in Los Andes
Imperialist mining is a central productive and hoarding process for the extended capital reproduction of the most advanced economic formations of the imperialism world-economy in competition for their inter-“nationalization".7 Although the institutional and military foundations of imperialist domination in Los Andes were settled with the implantation of the monopolistic finance capital of the most centralized bank capitalists state trusts of the U.S., through extractive enclaves on the most profitable industries in semi-feudal and semi-colonial “national-economies” during the period 1895 to 1930. From the beginning of the twentieth century, the mining industry in Los Andes was under the almost exclusive control of the U.S. imperialist bourgeoisie. Mining was one of the industries where capitalism developed most rapidly, especially in Los Andes, where from 1895 monopoly capital was directed toward large-scale mining, oil production, and agriculture (Mariategui [1928]2011:245-249; Cueva [1977]1999:63-64; Quijano [1977]1988:114-121).
In fact, the struggles of the central Peruvian highlands mining workers unions were executing active antisystemic resistance since their genesis with the first miners strike against the Cerro de Pasco Corporation (CPC) of January 7, 1919 -inspired in the advancements of the proletariat in Europe and the Soviet Revolution of October 1917 (Melgar Bao 2020:132)- until the end of the 1980s. The processes of union formation and politicization of the mining proletariat of the central highlands of Peru went through a trajectory of radicalization during most of the twentieth century. This trajectory of radicalization diffused to the medium and small mines at the national level, as well as to the mines of the Southern highlands -as was evidenced by the massacres of 1957 and 1966 in the mines of Toquepala (Moquegua), exploited by the Southern Peru Copper Corporation (SPC)-. Mines opened for the “nationalization" of the finance capital of the U.S. imperialist bourgeoisie through the Cuajone contract, secretly signed by the “nationalist" military government of Velasco Alvarado.8

Until the beginning of the Twenty First century, the rising tide of environmental struggles carried out by peasant mobilization against the imperialists mining bourgeoisies, takes particular relevance to the development of class struggle historical process, as the militarized dimension of capital accumulation is exhibited by the militarist policy executed against the indigenous-cholo antimining struggles in Los Andes. Antimining struggles require dedicated study and analysis of their effects on the political economy of imperialism, and as a priority field of contention for class struggle development in the Andean region considering the centrality of the mining circuit of capital reproduction during a period of shared imperialism breakout, ecological and productive systemic chaos.
Indeed, antimining struggles develop as limits to capital accumulation and imperialist domination in Los Andes. As O'Connor (1984) tried to remind to other radical thinkers in the middle of the 80's, Marxist theorists “left few clues regarding possible 'external barriers' to capitalist accumulation in the form of either 'limits to growth' determined by the availability of natural resources or [...] limits to accumulation determined by cultural-ideological conditions of economic and social reproduction” (O'Connor 1984:6). The indigenous-cholo antisystemic environmental-socialist struggles, as he would also state, are a central field of contention "to combat the attempt by capital and the state to ideologically construct and politically use the current crisis to restructure economic, political, and social life with the sole purpose of renewed capitalist accumulation, including a new 'long wave' of ideological innovation” (O'Connor 1984:23).
