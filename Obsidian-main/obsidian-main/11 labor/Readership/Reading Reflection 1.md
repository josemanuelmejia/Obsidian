Reading Reflection #8 Word Count: 869 What can we learn from those who oppose global power structures and how do they sustain oppression? My thoughts on two interesting works, the documentary Occupation 101: Voices of the Silenced Majority and the article Beyond the Economic Chaos of Coronavirus is a Global War Economy by William I. Robinson, are shaped by this question. Occupation 101 explores the Israeli-Palestinian conflict and reveals the destructive effects of occupation, while Robinson looks at how the epidemic has accelerated a military global economy that feeds on injustice and violence. Both pieces challenge me to face these systems and my place within them by exposing the processes of exploitation in our society while also highlighting the tenacity of individuals who continue in their battle for justice. Reading Robinson’s article was like having a veil lifted. He explains how the pandemic didn’t just expose inequality, it accelerated it. As millions of people lost jobs and struggled to survive, global elites used the chaos to grow their wealth and tighten their grip on power (Robinson, 2020). It was shocking to read about how this “war economy” depends on militarization, surveillance, and violence to keep itself going. I couldn’t help but think about how this has played out in real life. During the pandemic, while frontline workers were hailed as heroes, many were left with low pay and no safety net. At the same time, billionaires added trillions to their wealth. It’s infuriating to realize that these outcomes weren’t just bad luck or poor planning, they were the system working exactly as it was designed to. Robinson’s point that capitalism increasingly relies on violence and control to sustain itself felt both disturbing and undeniable. The documentary Occupation 101 brought these ideas to life in a way that was deeply personal and heartbreaking. It focuses on the lives of Palestinians under Israeli occupation, showing the daily indignities and systemic violence they endure. What struck me most were the stories of people who live under constant threat, families losing their homes, children navigating checkpoints, and entire communities struggling just to survive. Watching these stories, I felt both anger and helplessness. The documentary makes it clear that the occupation isn’t just about two groups in conflict; it’s a deliberate system of oppression backed by global powers. The U.S.’s financial and military support for Israel plays a huge role in maintaining this system. Seeing how intertwined global politics are with the suffering of real people was a sobering reminder of how power operates on an international scale. What also stood out to me was the way technology plays into this oppression. Advanced military equipment and surveillance tools are used to control Palestinians, and the occupied territories are often treated as a testing ground for these technologies. This reminded me of Robinson’s point about how tools developed for war are increasingly used to monitor and suppress civilian populations. It’s chilling to think about how these systems evolve and spread, affecting people far beyond their original context. Both Robinson’s article and Occupation 101 emphasize how global capitalism and militarism intersect to exploit people. Robinson talks about how capitalism thrives on inequality and uses militarized systems to maintain control, and Occupation 101 shows what this looks like on the ground in Palestine. Together, they made me realize how connected these systems are. The violence and oppression we see in one part of the world often feed into larger, global patterns. For example, Robinson mentions how crises like the pandemic become opportunities for elites to tighten their grip on power (Robinson, 2020). In Occupation 101, we see how the Israeli government uses similar tactics to justify its actions, framing oppression as necessary for “security.” Both works highlight how these narratives are used to protect systems of power while dehumanizing the people who suffer under them. Engaging with these works was both eye-opening and unsettling. They forced me to confront the fact that I’m not separate from these systems. Living in a country that benefits from global capitalism and militarism means I’m complicit in ways I hadn’t fully considered. Whether it’s through the taxes I pay, the products I buy, or even the media narratives I’ve accepted, I’ve played a part in sustaining these injustices. At the same time, these works reminded me that resistance is possible. Robinson ends his article with a call to action, emphasizing the importance of global movements to challenge these systems. Similarly, Occupation 101 highlights the resilience of Palestinians who continue to fight for their rights despite overwhelming odds. Their courage is a powerful reminder that change, while difficult, is always worth striving for. These works left me with a deeper understanding of how interconnected the world’s injustices are—and how necessary it is to address them. Challenging the global war economy means tackling inequality, dismantling systems of violence, and amplifying the voices of those who are most affected. For me, this means staying informed, questioning the systems I participate in, and finding ways to support movements for justice. Whether it’s through education, advocacy, or direct action, there are ways to resist. Both Robinson’s article and Occupation 101 showed me that while the systems shaping our world are powerful, so is the human capacity for resilience and change.
