\title{Antisystemic Effects of Antimining Andean Peasant Struggles}

\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
The metabolic structural processes of the capitalist world-economy have produced historical antisystemic resistances with world-scale effects over capital's extended reproduction. The history of the Andean indigenous and cholo peasantry struggles against colonial and imperialist mining constituted a new political identity in absolute contradiction with the dispossession and destruction of land and water as vital values for their social reproduction. While theory has been developed about the antisystemic effects of social movements created by the systemic processes of the world-economy, theorizing on their causal mechanisms is a work in progress, permeable to be developed broadly within a Marxist critique of imperialism political economy. In this article, a causal mechanisms hypothesis is built to explain the world-scale effects of antimining struggles over the finance capital reproduction circuit of mining imperialists bourgeoisies in competition under the gradual breakout conditions of shared imperialism in Los Andes from three case studies: i) the cholo-peasantry antimining struggles in Cajamarca (Peru) during the 2004 and 2011-2012 against the Newmont Gold Mining Corporation headed in the U.S., ii) the indigenous-peasantry mining struggles in Apurímac (Peru) during 2015 and 2019 against the MMG of China Minmetals Corporation (CMC), and iii) the indigenous-cholo peasantry antimining struggles in Cotopaxi (Ecuador) during 2023-2024 against the Atico Mining Corporation headed in Canada. The theory-building case studies conducted have been nurtured by fieldwork, archive research, and protest event data analysis to evidence the consistency of the hypothesis stated on the antisystemic effects of antimining struggles.
\end{abstract}

En este artículo, construyo una hipótesis de mecanismos causales para explicar los efectos antisistémicos de las luchas antimineras sobre la reproducción del capital financiero circulado en la industria minera por burguesías imperialistas en competencia bajo condiciones de ruptura progresiva del imperialismo compartido en Los Andes, a partir de tres estudios de caso: i) las luchas antimineras del campesinado cholo en Cajamarca (Perú) durante los años 2004 y 2011-2012 contra la Newmont Gold Mining Corporation asentada en EE.UU., ii) las luchas del campesinado indígena en Apurímac (Perú) durante 2015 y 2019 contra la MMG de la China Minmetals Corporation (CMC), y iii) las luchas antimineras campesinas cholo-indígenas en Cotopaxi (Ecuador) durante 2023-2024 contra la canadiense Atico Mining Corporation. \section{Introduction}
The effects of the antisystemic resistance of the antimining movement in Los Andes express the advance of the development of the class struggle in the identity formation of historical challengers in absolute contradiction (Lenin 1914; Anderson and Hudis 2007) to the reproduction of the imperialists bourgeoisies finance capital through the mining circuit of accumulation.
Mining in Los Andes is at the center of the foundational processes of the modern world-system and in the metabolic processes of reproduction of finance capital in the current world-economy. The development of the processes of production and circulation of colonial and imperialist mining, and gold mining in particular, have structured the social formation and historical resistance of the indigenous-cholo peasantry, against militarism, dispossession and expansive destruction of the vital values for their social reproduction: water and land.

This is evidenced by the genocide of the “indigenous” peoples of Abya Yala, the exploitation of the survivors in colonial mining, as well with the massacres of their proletarianized children and grandchildren indigenous-cholo peasants with the imperialist implantation of U.S. monopolist capitalists trusts’ finance capital inter-“nationalized” in mining enclaves in Los Andes. Militarism as a policy of expansion of the imperialists bourgeoisies to control or guarantee the functioning of the mining circuit for the reproduction of their finance capital, is a persistent feature of the coloniality of the current mining problem. As well as a nodal point of his antiimperialist solution in Los Andes. In the origine of the formation of the modern world-system that began in the sixteenth century and in the world-economy of imperialism development during the late nineteenth and early twentieth century, the massacres and exploitation of the indigenous-cholo proletarianized peasantry in the mines of Los Andes operated as a constant under colonial mining and imperialist mining.

Colonial mining in Los Andes was the foundational method of the expanded original accumulation of the sixteenth century (Cueva [1977]1999:63–78; Marx [1886]1976:874-875,915) corresponding to the material expansion of the European oligarchic banking bourgeoisies (Arrighi 1994) that emerged from the concentration of merchant capital in the Middle East during the thirteenth century (Abu-Lughod 1991), shaping the modern world-system. Imperialist mining is a vital process of production and hoarding for the extended reproduction of finance capitals with differentiated trajectories of centralization and concentration (Bukharin [1929]1966) guaranteeing their inter-“nationalization” through militarism (Luxemburg [1913]2015).

In their effort to conceptualize the antisystemic effects of the movements produced by the systemic structural processes of the world-economy, Arrighi, Hopkins, and Wallerstein (1989:90) treated the antisystemic effects as caused by the centralization of capital as a world-scale tendency highly developed under the world-economy of imperialism, and highlighted the divergence of the potentially challenging [new] social sites/footnote{“Social sites consists of loci in which organized human action occurs; they include individuals, aspects of individuals, organizations, networks, and places” (Tilly 2002:xi). As the events presented, the emergence of new challengers with not only abstract but absolute antagonistic identities appears as reminders of the development of the history of class struggle. We build a hypothesis of causal mechanisms to answer the question: How the anti-mining struggles produce anti-systemic effects on the reproduction of capital by the imperialist bourgeoisies? We argue that the antisystemic effects of historical challengers formed by the expansive structural processes vital for the reproduction of competing finance capitals under conditions of shared imperialism in Los Andes, are real in their consequences.

The effects of antimining struggles on the metabolic mechanisms of capital reproduction are antisystemic, also because its absolute antagonist identity exacerbates the imperialistic competence for material expansion facing a forthcoming re-partition imperialists wars.

\section{Antisystemic resistance of historical challengers: the antimining movement of the indigenous-cholo Andean peasantry}

The formation of the class structure of the modern world-system is founded on the racialized international division of labor produced by colonization as a policy of expansion of merchant capital. Around ten million “indigenous" survived the genocide planned in Los Andes of an estimated total of sixty five million exterminated over the continental “America", being sublimated as inferior beings and reduced to mere labor force by the colonization method of primitive accumulation (Marx [1886]1976:873–875, 914; Mariátegui, January 16, 1929:78; Quijano and Wallerstein 1992; Quijano [1999], 2007, 169-170; Mignolo 2011, 183–84).

The world no longer fit into the consciousness of the survivors. But in their collective consciousness a myth survived. Or a myth makes sense in our consciousness, it is the myth of the new indigenous, of the Inkarri (the Inka return), of the Yawar-Inti (Sun of Blood) and the resurgence of the “indigenous" world by a revolutionary vendetta. In opposition to Arboleda's (2020) interpretation of the formation of indigenous “identity politics"\footnote{Martín Arboleda stated: ““Already in the 1990s, before socalled “identity politics” gained prominence in international academic circles, Ecuadorian philosopher Bolívar Echeverría —a renowned theoretician of cultural syncretism and capitalist modernity in Latin America— was already puzzled by the self-defeating cultural essentialisms of emerging variants of Latin American indigenous politics. Pre-Hispanic cultures in the Americas, he argued, were undergirded by profoundly relational cosmologies and hence were unable to perceive the “Other” as independent alterity. The otherness they perceived in the Spanish colonizers was for them a mere variant of the sameness of their own collective self. The European mindset, on the other hand, always construed the cultural other in terms of radical alterity, as absolute negation of their own identity” (Arboleda 2020:210).}, I argue that the revolutionary subjectivity that conjures up indigenous socialism is a historical formation in movement, a block of living historical text clashing, developing the peasantry as an antagonist class in formal subsumption since the sixteenth century. 

For both Arboleda (2020) and Bolívar Echevarría, the indigenous peoples could not understand the antagonism between them and the Spaniards. Their cosmology, they believe, did not allow the indigenous to realize that they were being killed by an external collective antagonistic to their lives or “collective self." If someone follows that argument, by implication this indigenous “self-defeating cultural essentialisms" would only change with the later appropriation of Hegelian logic as the basement for the reproduction of the sublimating rationality of “European mentality". By implication, its since the encounter of the “indigenous" peoples with this “European mentality" that their descendants are able to interpret “the cultural other in terms of radical otherness, as an absolute negation of its own identity". In short, it must be understood that Bolívar Echevarría should have ideologically omitted the work of the Peruvian anthropologist Luis E. Valcárcel, so as not to force an approximation to the work of José Carlos Mariátegui, who found an indigenous revolutionary subject already in motion when they were going to work on its politicization at the beginning of the twentieth century. Fortunately, the work of Alberto Flores Galindo ([1985]2010) did justice to this problem by tracing the myth of the Inkarri in indigenous and popular struggles emblematic of the history of Peru. 

As the first worker-intellectuals became the prophets of this myth, indigenous socialism was forged in the early twentieth century as the convergence of the “cholo group"\footnote{The “cholo group" is a social stratum which formation arises from the mass of the servile or semi-servile indigenous peasantry, differentiated by the incorporation of elements of Western-Creole culture. However, at the same time, they sustain, preserve, and recognize their difference and their origins in the indigenous culture and in the social structure of the indigenous peasantry, as the social formation from which we depart (Quijano 1980:63-65).} with the indigenous peasantry in historical resistance. The work of socialist politicization undertaken by Mariátegui, Mayer and Valcárcel (among others) in the organization of the bases of socialism in Peru and in the creation of indigenous socialism, they found already in motion the myth of indigenous resurgence. In which framework of interpretation, the revolution is carried out as vendetta for the new indigenous, their remnants, their sons and grandsons. The socialist revolution as a movement and horizon was already operating in Los Andes, when El Amauta José Carlos Mariátegui and the cholo group which he constituted with other intellectual workers of the proletariat in formation, began their work of politicization. 

Mariátegui wrote in the prologue to Valcárcel's Tempest in Los Andes (1927): “I remember the unexpected and impressive type of agitator I found four years ago in the indigenous from Puno, Ezequiel Urviola. This meeting was the greatest surprise that Peru had in store for me on my return from Europe. Urviola represented the first spark of a fire that was to come. He was the revolutionary indigenous, the socialist indigenous. Tuberculous, hunchbacked, he succumbed after two years of tireless work. Today it does not matter that Urviola does not exist. It is enough that he existed. As Valcárcel says, today the mountains are pregnant with spartacus" (Mariátegui in the prologue to Valcárcel 1927:11).
The history of the class struggle in Los Andes definitely precedes socialism, but most important, the indigenous-cholo peasantry learned to instrumentalize it. Thus, by the first quarter of the twenty-first century, the indigenous-cholo peasantry has not only effectively instrumentalized socialism (and Marxism) but also environmentalism (Mejía 2021; 2024), fixing its discursive articulation on the recognition of sensitive entities such as mountains, rivers, and lagoons (de la Cadena 2010). As De la Cadena emphasized about the visionary work of José María Arguedas: “[t]he totality, Todas las Sangres proposed an alternative indigenous social movement, a critical ally of the modern left, but with its own a-modern hybrid logic. Literacy and modern politics were important, but they had to be used and translated selectively, rather than eradicating indigenous customs" (2005:18). 

Hybridity in this sense is performative and not a reproduction of Western modernization as acculturation, but rather the cholos as indigenous descendants have been building their own cultural logic that recognizes their origins, while appropriating Western-modern cultural objects, as we do with the socialist and ecological frames of interpretation in the mining and mining struggles of the twenty-first century. As evidence the three case studies: i) the cholo-peasantry antimining struggles in Cajamarca (Peru) during the 2004 and 2011-2012 against the Newmont Gold Mining Corporation headed in the U.S., ii) the indigenous-peasantry mining struggles in Apurímac (Peru) during 2015 and 2019 against the MMG of China Minmetals Corporation (CMC), and iii) the indigenous-cholo peasantry antimining struggles in Cotopaxi (Ecuador) during 2023-2024 against the Atico Mining Corporation headed in Canada. The cholo-indigenous peasantry updated their interpretation of mining from a class perspective, nurtred by ecological discourses and indigenous cosmopolitics (de la Cadena 2020) in the defense of water, land, life and biodiversity. 

\subsection{The Historical Resistance of the Cholo-Indigenous Peasant Struggles Against Imperialist Mining in Los Andes}

Imperialist mining is a central productive and hoarding process for the extended capital reproduction of the bank state capitalists trusts\footnote{“The development of joint  stock companies, which made it possible to use the capital of  a considerable number of individual entrepreneurs, and which  radically undermined the principle of individual ownership of  enterprises, created the prerequisites for large monopolistic associations of entrepreneurs. Concentration of capital assumed  a new form here, namely, the form of concentration in trusts. Capital accumulation no more increased the capital of individual producers; it turned into a means of increasing the capital of entrepreneurs’ organizations.[...] The individual  production branches are in various ways knit together into one collective body, organized on a large scale. Finance capital seizes the entire country in an iron grip. “National economy” turns into one gigantic combined trust whose partners are the financial groups [banks] and the state. Such formations we call state capitalist trusts" (Bukharin [1915]1988:117-118).}, as the most advanced economic formations of the world-economy\footnote{“[W]orld  economy includes all these economic phenomena which, all  in all, are based on the relations between human beings engaged in the process of production. By and large, the whole  process of world economic life in modern times reduces itself  to the production of surplus value and its distribution among the various groups and sub-groups of the bourgeoisie on the  basis of an ever widening reproduction of the relations between two classes —the class of the world proletariat on the one hand and the world bourgeoisie on the other" (Bukharin [1915]1988:26-27).} of imperialism in competition for their inter-“nationalization".\footnote{This concept is widely explained in the next section.}
Although the institutional and military foundations of imperialist domination in Los Andes were settled with the implantation of the monopolistic finance capital of the most centralized capitalists trusts of the U.S., through extractive enclaves for the most profitable industries in semi-feudal and semi-colonial “national-economies" during the period 1895 to 1930. From the beginning of the twentieth century, the mining industry in Los Andes was under the almost exclusive control of the U.S. imperialist bourgeoisie. Mining was one of the industries where capitalism developed most rapidly, especially in Los Andes, where from 1895 monopoly capital was directed toward large-scale mining, oil production, and agriculture (Mariátegui [1928]2011:245-249; Cueva [1977]1999:63-64; Quijano [1977]1988:114-121).

In fact, the struggles of the central Peruvian highlands mining workers unions were executing active antisystemic resistance since their genesis with the first miners strike against the Cerro de Pasco Corporation (CPC) of January 7, 1919 -inspired in the advancements of the proletariat in Europe and the Soviet Revolution of October 1917 (Melgar Bao, 2020:132)- until the end of the 1980s.  The processes of union formation and politicization of the mining proletariat of the central highlands of Peru went through a trajectory of radicalization during most of the twentieth century. This trajectory of radicalization diffused to the medium and small mines at the national level, as well as to the mines of the southern highlands –as was evidenced by the massacres of 1957 and 1966 in the mines of Toquepala (Moquegua), exploited by the Southern Peru Copper Corporation (SPC)–. Mines opened for the “nationalization" of the finance capital of the U.S. imperialist bourgeoisie through the Cuajone contract, secretly signed by the “nationalist" military government of Velasco Alvarado.\footnote{The Southern Peru Copper Corporation (SPC) was founded in 1952 by the American Mining and Smelting Company (ASARCO), which at the first decade of the 1900s operated the mining reproduction circuit of the finance capital bearers, as the Phelps Dodge Overseas Capital Company, the Cerro de Pasco Corporation (CPC), and the Newmont Mining Company, which concentrated already by 1901 20 percent of copper melting and 15 percent of copper refinement at a world-scale (Pérez-Jiménez, 2020:25). Evidencing the continuum of exclusive monopolistic operation of the U.S. imperialist bourgeoisies centralized formations in Peru during the complete twentieth century.}

While during the 1980s, the co-optation of the mining workers unions of the Southern Peru Copper Corporation (SPC) was consolidated as a result of the enforcement of its institutionalization trajectory. Meanwhile, the intensity and harshness of the militarism executed against the protests of the mining workers in the central Peruvian highlands exploited by the Cerro de Pasco Copper Corporation (CPC), evidenced the disruptiveness of the transgression carried out by the mining workers struggles as a direct challenge to the main reproduction circuit of capital accumulation. The systematic application of militarism is the sole guarantee to hold its imperialist policy over the mines of the central highlands of Peru was evidenced in the massacres of Malpaso (1930), Cobriza (1971), as well as the militarized repression of the miners' strike campaign of the mid-80s repressed with the imprisonment of hundreds of unionized miners, and the assassination of Saúl Cantoral in 1988 by the “Commando Rodrigo Franco" task force (a paramilitary criminal organization for annihilation that operated under the first government of Alan García).

However, the radicalization of the miners' strikes developed facing militarized repression and military regimes. The formation of a vanguard of the Peruvian proletariat in open confrontation with the U.S. imperialist mining bourgeoisie, developed until the 1980s, when the Communist Party of Peru-Sendero Luminoso (PCP-SL) and the Tupac Amaru Revolutionary Movement (MRTA) began armed struggles. The trajectory of radicalization of the struggles of the miners became an anti-imperialist challenge, operated in a context characterized by the escalation of the armed struggle in Latin America, by the consequences of the Sino-Soviet secession, and the resolution of the imperialists wars between the U.S.S.R. and the U.S.. The formation of the basis for the horizontal centralization of Sino-American finance capital developed, gradually after the shared imperialist resolution of the Vietnam War, and the subsequent implementation of market reforms in China by Deng XiaoPing in 1978, until the early years of the 21st century, marked by the September 11 of 2001 attacks on Wall Street, the Pentagon and the White House. When, under conditions of shared imperialism, these financial capitals begin a process of separation and rupture, gradually reducing their horizontal centralization. Thus, constraining the inter-“nationalization" of U.S. bank capitals as finance capital circulating through the economic formations of China's industrial production.

Indeed, U.S. and China imperialists bourgeoisies share the conditions of accumulation for their mutually consented capital concentration and inter-“nationalization" in the period between the 70s and the first decade of the 2000s, with a breaking point in the years 2013-2015, denoting a initial disentangle of their rates of profit, that could be extended mildly until 2019, with a recent affirmation between 2021 and 2023, which supposed to be reinforced with the updated data which considers the 2024 interstate contention. Considering its estimation as a proxy to the monetized-capital amount accountable for the exchange of gold-money-capital reserves as a reserve fund of surplus-value congealed under the shape of gold-commodity-capital and gold-money-capital at the same time (Figure 4).

The finance capital advanced inter-“nationalization" of a highly centralized China state capitalists trust during the 2000s, gradually took advantage of the institutional and military conditions established by the U.S. imperialists bourgeoisies to operate in Peru. In twenty years, China's state capitalists trust finance capital built a considerable expansion of its participation in Peruvian copper mining, although still incipient in gold mining, through the China Minmetals Corporation (CMC). The differentiated imperialists mining bourgeoisies are in the core of the inter-“nationalized" organization of finance capital, subjugating the industrial mining production of the semi-colonial “national-economies" in Los Andes to the whole circuit of reproduction of the highest economic formations resultant from differentiated trajectories of centralization at a “national-economy" scale. \section{Antimining struggles blocking the expansion of the U.S. gold mining extractive frontier in Cajamarca}

The expansion of the extractive frontier of Minera Yanacocha-Newmont in Cajamarca has been contested by an mining movement that employs what is called a unique front strategy. It has proven to be effective in creating sufficient people’s social power to confront imperialist mining. The harshness of the military’s repression against the popular and mainly peasant mining movement in Cajamarca revealed the cruelty of the imperialist domination in Peru, just after the democratic election of a president of nationalist and military background (Ollanta Humala). The mining struggles in Cajamarca taught us that peasant struggles can be interpreted and developed as anti-imperialist struggles, since they imply a class confrontation between two extremes of capitalist accumulation: the international mining bourgeoisie and the indigenous peasantry. The challenge that mining struggles represent for the central nucleus of imperialist domination in countries where mining constitutes the main enclave of capitalist accumulation, points to the strategic nature of the mining struggles. They are the spearhead for the development of an antisystemic movement, as well as critical for the geo-economics needs of the dynamics of capital accumulation during the current systemic crisis (Veltmeyer 2015:124; Wallerstein 2002:38).

The mining struggles for the defense of Quilish Mountain and the water sources of Cajamarca in 2004 had shown the effectiveness of the unique front strategy: they have joined the efforts of multiple organizations. These include: peasant nightwatch organizations (“rondas campesinas”), irrigation boards (“juntas de riego”), urban nightwatch organizations (“rondas urbanas”), the teachers union at the provincial and district level (SUTEP-Cajamarca), fractions of the PCP-PR of Cajamarca, and university students and environmental NGOs such as Ecovida and Grufides.
Since the beginning of the struggle against the Conga mining project of Minera Yanacocha-Newmont in November 2011, the environmental defense fronts confronted the government of the recently elected president Ollanta Humala who had promised to the peasants that Cajamarca’s lagoons would not be destroyed nor would mining be allowed without popular consent.

In this context, the sustained mining campaign opposing Minera Yanacocha-Newmont's Conga project escalated, contesting not only to the U.S. gold mining company, but directly the administration and government of the former military captain and President Ollanta Humala. The formation of the environmental defense fronts at the beginning of the first decade of the 2000s in Cajamarca was the result of a political coalition between the environmental NGOs –particularly Ecovida–, the teachers provincial union (SUTEP Cajamarca) and the peasant nightwatch organizations then controlled by the PCP-PR, as an effective strategy to unite the popular forces in defense of Cajamarca's water sources. However, after some time this coalition lost control of the popular mining movement.

In this context, a new group of leaders, composed of radical professional politicians, garnered control of the movement, limiting the role of the PCP-PR and the environmental NGOs.
Thus, in September of 2010, Celendin's first declaration was made to create the Interprovincial Coordinator for the Defense of the Environment, which forged a new strong coalition for mining struggles including second-level organizations of five provinces located in the direct influence area of the Conga mining project (Interview LM2 03/06/2014). This coalition would allow coordination between the environmental NGOs, led by Marco Arana (GRUFIDES), and the radical fraction of the popular movement of Cajamarca led by Wilfredo Saavedra. As the latter was gaining strength among the peasant and urban bases of Cajamarca, bringing together fractions of the teacher’s union (SUTEP Cajamarca) and peasant and urban nightwatch organizations (“rondas campesinas y urbanas”) opposed to the control of the PCP-PR, the frictions between these sectors worsened.

With the formation of this new coalition, the sector of Wilfredo Saavedra would be in charge of learning and translating the environmental framework to a broader sector of the peasantry, university students and neighborhood organizations. This demonstrated the capacity of the radical sector leadership to further broaden the bases of the unique front against the expansion of the extractive frontier of Minera Yanacocha-Newmont. The radicalization of the struggle began on November 24, 2011, when the peasants of Bambamarca and Celendín took over the lagoons that the Conga Mining Project planned to destroy. This performance of struggle required an effective organizational structure that would allow the mobilization and rotation of 200 peasants every two days to prevent the machinery of Minera Yanacocha, the police and the military from entering the lagoons and taking control of water sources (Interview LM5 03/08/2014).

The magnitude of the repression carried out by the government of Ollanta Humala crudely demonstrates the operation of mining accumulation by repression against the peasantry of Cajamarca. The militarization of Cajamarca was order by decree RS 231-2012-DE, which imposed the state of emergency from May 29 to June 25, 2012 in the provinces of Cajamarca, Celendín and Hualgayoc.

In this context, on July 3, 2012, during the 34th day of the regional strike (“paro regional indefinido”) in Cajamarca, the military forces killed four peasants in the town’s square of Celendín:

“Here, on July 3, it happened that the police and the military were down on the corner [..] when they started shooting at us, then the people who were here in the central square [of Celendín] began to run through different places […]; and the people […] took shelter here in the church of the Virgen del Carmen. \section{Obstructing China’s copper mining under imperialist U.S. domination in Apurímac}

The cruelty of the imperialist repression against the mining workers during the 20th century reveals the magnitude of the repression of the mining struggle carried out by the peasantry today, after a century of political mining contention in Peru. The social formation that currently directly confronts extractive mining capital is the peasantry, not the mining proletariat. By claiming the defense of water, and and life against the extension of the extractive frontier, mining struggles show themselves to be absolutely antagonistic to the extractive capitalist accumulation process. That is, as a frontal struggle against the international mining bourgeoisie, and even beyond, the mining struggle confronts the capitalist system and the productive and commercial dynamics that sustain it on a global scale, blocking its reproduction. Faced with this problem, imperialist domination mobilized the armed military forces to enforce the interests of the mining bourgeoisie (Lust 2016:5).

On Friday, October 14, 2016, two hundred indigenous peasants from the communities of Choquecca, Quehuira, Allahua, Antuyo and Pumamarca, located in the province of Cotabambas (Apurímac), began a road blockade obstructing the trucks loaded with mineral ore through the mining road used by the Chinese capital company MMG Limited Las Bambas. The peasant communities demanded payment from the mining company for the use of a communal road within their territories, through which the mine site connects to the main mining road and the trucks loaded with mineral ore head to the port of Matarani in the region of Arequipa. The road blockade that started that morning was brutally cleared by the police, killing community member Quintino Cereceda Huiza with a bullet to the head and wounding twenty more peasants. A few days later, the Minister of the Interior (security) Carlos Basombrío, declared that the police forces were not authorized in any way to clear the road blockade that day (El Comercio 2016:8).

The communities of southern Cotabambas rebelled against the power of the Chinese mining company MMG Limited Las Bambas for 186 days until April 17, 2017, when the province of Cotabambas was assigned the state of emergency, authorizing military repression against community members. Although the mining struggle of the peasant communities of Cotabambas (Apurímac) against the Chinese mining company MMG Limited Las Bambas did not include expressly an "antimining" claim -as in Cajamarca and Cotopaxi- its direct target is the imperialist mining company, and indeed the magnitude of the repression executed to secure the interests of China’s mining bourgeoisie confirms the disposition of the armed forces to serve in shared imperialism conditions to guarantee the MMG mining operations by militarism.

The article shows that antimining struggles have an antisystemic effect on the mining circuit of extended capital accumulation, and effectively challenge imperialist domination in Peru. 
By blocking the expansion of mining’s territorial frontier and obstructing the production and circulation of mining commodities, mining struggles challenge the mining bourgeoisie and the circulation of its productive capital.

The development of the political identity which started to take form during the first years of the 2000s in the firsts antimining campaigns of struggle executed by the effective mobilization of the peasantry organizations in Cajamarca (Peru) under the strategy of the constitution of a unique front for the defense of the water and land against mining dispossession and destruction, by the diffusion of an environmental frame open to its appropriation by the indigenous-cholo peasantry organizations as Las Rondas Campesinas, socialist leaderships as the socialist vanguard which radicalized the antimining front during 2010 and 2012, left unions as the SUTEP, subnational parties and electoral politicians as the MAS of Cajamarca and the former subnational governor Gregorio Santos.

The effectiveness of obstructive mining performances in Peru was revealed at least since 2004, with the success of the mining protests against Minera Yanacocha-Newmont and the withdrawal of government authorization to explore the Quilish mountain in Cajamarca. The increasingly radical nature of the contentious performances by the peasants of Huambocancha Alta and Huambocancha Baja achieved an uninterrupted blockade of the access route to Minera Yanacocha-Newmont gold mine, and stalled the police forces sent to unblock it. Although  it wasn’t the first time that highway was blocked during episodes of mining contention in Cajamarca, it is worth saying that such the blockade leads to the gradual stoppage of the mine’s gold mining activities, significantly affecting its production and profits (Mejía 2021:97).

Ross (2003:54, 62) points out that minerals are a type of natural resource whose transport is relatively  easy to obstruct, which has implications for the duration and intensity of extractive conflicts. The blockade implied not only the impossibility of access and exit to the mine’s facilities, but also the expensive use of helicopters for the supply of fuel, equipment, labor and other supplies (Pérez Mundaca 2012:70-71). Thus, the obstructive performances blocking the Cajamarca-Bambamarca highway ended up drowning Minera Yanacocha-Newmont, proving to be an effective performance of struggle for almost the entire duration of the mining campaign for the defense of Quilish mountain (Mejía 2021:98-99).

Innovative obstructive performances shared in Apurímac and Cajamarca highlight how imperialist mining operates in Los Andes. The companies of the international mining bourgeoisies need to establish the flow of the extracted mineral either to a smelting plant -such as the Southern Peru Copper Corporation has in Moquegua-, or directly to the port of Matarani in Arequipa. As suggested by Ye et al. (2020:7), mining multinational corporations claim the monopolized control of the territory and its resources, but also of the entry and exit points from which mineral commodities are transported to a port for exportation, finally traded for export, where it completes its conversion into money-capital. In this sense, mining and mining struggles in Los Andes effectively block the expansion of the extractive mining frontier and obstruct the circuit of the imperialist bourgeoisies finance capital invested in mining. The indigenous peasantry is actually at the forefront of antisystemic movements challenging the reproduction of global capitalism to defend their water, their lands, and their “collective self” from imperialist mining.

\subsection{The indigenous-cholo peasantry struggles against imperialist mining as an antisystemic movement}

The antisystemic historical resistance in Los Andes is indigenous-cholo in its broad base, structurally placed in subordination by the negative dialectics of race, which created the international division of labor by the material expansion of the competitive semi-colonial policy for mining exploitation, trade, and hoarding of merchant capitals in its attempted transition toward the formation of the oligarchic banking bourgeoisies which held the modern world-system under four successive Western inner-transitions since the XVI century.

Antimining struggles in Los Andes antagonize these reproduction processes and mechanisms vital for the modern world-system, and the imperialist world-economy. If Andean mining struggles decreases the imperialist mining bourgeoisies' rate of profit, it's antisystemic effects should have differentiated consequences on the multilevel polity contention among the top-layer of the imperialist bourgeoisies ruling the U.S. and the top-layer of the imperialist bourgeoisie ruling China for the expansion of its circuit of capital accumulation. At the current breakdown of the modern world-system shared imperialism of Sino-American international bank state capitalists trusts, due to its capital horizontal centralization initiated during the '70s and held until the 2019, when began its horizontal decentralization. The horizontal decentralization process is expressed in the protectionist policies adopted by both imperialists bourgeoisies due to an increasing digression of their rate of profit (Figura 2 y Figura 3). During the breakdown of shared imperialism conditions, differentiated accumulation regimes ruling their capital concentration and centralization dynamics are sensible to the disruption of the circuit of capital reproduction in the strategic industries over which the higher rate of profit are made. Mining and particular, gold mining in Los Andes are core, strategic, metabolic, processes of capital production and circulation, Are structural processes of the world-economy of imperialism, the core of the circuit of the capital reproduction of the international bank state capitalists trusts, as are their finance capital and its imperialist bourgeoisies (including its domestic managers, the bureaucratic bourgeoisies) the ones operating the mines, and the militarism with which they realize its exploitation.

Indeed, the differentiated reproduction outcomes depend on the natural and cultural conditions, expressed in the organic composition of capital at each instance of the whole circuit of capital reproduction. The differentiation mechanism operating at the level of the rate of profit depends on the transformation of surplus-value into profit at a concrete time of circulation, and expected proportions of variable and constant capital consumption, given by the natural, cultural, and -as I argue here- by the historical conditions. Manifested in the Andean indigenous-cholo antimining struggles, as historical challengers formed with the constitution of the modern world-system and the foundational and metabolic --gold-mining--reproduction processes of the capitalist world-economy.

The antimining peasants’ struggles in Los Andes of South America effectively obstruct the conversion of the money invested as mining productive capital by different imperialist bourgeoisies in competition for the extension of its reproduction circuit scale. Employing its militarized policy over the territories cursed by the abundance of mineral commodities under different forms of militarism, as military and paramilitary interventions in Latin America, or as new imperialist wars as initially expressed in Ukraine and Palestine (or sooner Taiwan) ongoing multilevel polity conflicts.

The processes of production and circulation of mining imperialist productive capital had been challenged by proletarian mining workers' struggles in China at the coal and lead mines of Anyuan, Pinghsiang County (Kiangsi Province) and Shuikoushan, Changning (Hunan Province) between 1921 and 1927; as well as in Peru in episodes of mining contention identified as the massacres of Malpaso (1930), Toquepala (1966), and Cobriza (1971). Nevertheless, the cooperation between the peasants' movement and the proletarian mining workers in China developed in the Autumn Harvest Uprising of 1927. On Octorber of 1928, Mao Tse-Tung led this forces to the Chingkang Mountains, and the further armed struggle against Japanese and U.S. imperialism and shared imperialism (Mao, [1928]1967:63-69, 81-103).
In contrast, the extension of the peasants-workers revolutionary coalition is a matter of polemic between historians of the peasants' movement and mining worker's struggles in Peru. The development of the peasantry as a class for itself sparked at the final decades of the XX century, the indigenous uprising in Ecuador of 1990 and the Chiapas liberation by the EZLN in 1994, which preceded the resistance against the expansion of the mining extractive frontier accelerated since the first years of the XXI century.

Antimining struggles are historically rooted in the historical problem of land dispossession and indigenous socialism within the Andean peasantry. Economic claims are grounded in the survival of the \textit{quechua, kichwa, aymara, wankas, caranquis, cañaris, otavalos, pastos} in the highlands, \textit{moches, valdivias, huancavilcas, afros} in the coastal valleys, \textit{mapuches} in the southern forests, and \textit{awajún, waoranis, cofanes, secoyas, shuaras, achuras, zaparas, among others} in the Amazonian, the ankebain of Mamansoya (and the other inhabitants of other seven worlds connected with this (Ortiz Rescaniere, 1975)), survived to the colonization genocide of the original accumulation process in Los Andes. Culturally,  materially, historically, the resistance of the "indigenous" peoples of Los Andes, reproduced and developed the intrinsic relation between the "indigenous" and the "land".\footnote{““The land,” Valcárcel writes in his study of the economic life of Tawantinsuyu, in native tradition, is the common mother: not only food but man himself comes from her womb. Land provides all wealth. The cult of the Mama Pacha, Mother Earth, is on par with the worship of the sun, and such as the sun does not belong to anyone in particular, neither does the planet. Agrarianism was born from the twin concepts of aboriginal ideology of communal ownership of land and the universal
religion of the sun" (Mariátegui, 1928[2011]:72-73). In this manner the spontaneous and the free association of indigenous communities was possible to conceal with the existence of a ruling state class (Inkas) because of the different meaning that collective work have for the indigenous communities as something which is not sufferable, inclusive nowadays the community work in indigenous communities is more a collective dance with the soil than a mechanic activity for exhaustion and extraction, and its full of meaning and rituals. The indigenous communism is alive in the spirit of its sons and grandsons, detached from land by the violence of original accumulation and its sequels well known in Los Andes as massacres. The demonstration of the survival of indigenous socialism is not only in the “vestiges” left by the survival of indigenous communities, but also in the different types of chholo indigenous peasantry communities which appears in the development of the semi-feudal condition and capitalist expansion as a whole process: 
“Therefore, in indigenous villages where families are grouped and bonds of heritage and communal work have been extinguished, strong and tenacious habits of cooperation and solidarity that are the empirical expression of a communist spirit still exist. The community draws on this spirit. It is their body. When expropriation and redivision seem about to liquidate the community, Indigenous socialism always finds a way to reject, resist, or evade it.” (Mariátegui, 1928[2011]:98).
Mariátegui's approach to the indigenous problem as the land problem, is oriented not only to challenge the oligarchy of landowners with military coalitions in control of land in Los Andes; but also to target the bureaucratic bourgeoisie, and in that manner attacks the political structure of imperialism.} 

The dispossession and destruction of vital values for the social reproduction of the indigenous-cholo peasantry and the rural proletariat as the social formations entangled with the land and water, by the expansion of the mining extractive frontier is irreconcilable with the existence not only of the peasantry but for the reproduction of the proletariat surrounding the imperialist mining enclave.\footnote{The size and quality of the mines opened in Los Andes located in volcanic geological formation are abundant in copper and gold, mainly, and the perforations required for the exploitation of the minerals evidence the liberation of arsenic into the underground water sources, permeating the soil as the main epidemiological structural threat of contamination with extremely compared magnitudes in Latin America (Bundschuh et al., 2011).} The territories cursed by the mining industry are object of total biodiversity extermination due to the destruction of the conditions for life in general, and not only the cultural or natural conditions for the reproduction of peasant economies.

Although past mining contention waves of antisystemic resistance occurred during transitional stages of the capitalist modern world-system development -i.e. as the mining proletariat struggles developed in Peru and Great Britain during the first quarter of the 1900s-, the current restructuring attempt of the modern capitalist world-economy not only depend on a completed transition in the afterward of U.S. hegemony breakdown. Since the liberalization of the gold price (Figure 1) in 1971, the extension of the mining extractive frontier particularly of gold production motivated by the demand of monetary gold holdings for national reserve’s banks, raised exponentially since 2001. 

The tendency to accumulate monetary gold in national reserves that was strongly monopolistic under U.S. hegemony until the beginning of the new millennia, when it began to be followed not only by the European central banks of Germany, France and Italy, but also by Russia, India, Japon, and specifically China.
The last mentioned bank state capitalist trust increased its acquisition of gold for its national reserves, as its superiority in monetary holdings compared to the U.S. (Figure 2) required a balance in proportion to its monetary-gold holdings (Figure 3) to overcome the U.S. dollar as hegemonic currency, and intensified its capital production by gold mining in national contexts under which the situation of Sino-American shared imperialism remains, as in Latin America.

The total reserves of China in 2021 (3,43 trillion US dollars) are 4.79 times the volume of total reserves of the U.S. for the same year (716,15 billion US dollars) (Fig.1). However, the U.S. total gold reserves for the same last year 2021 was of 475,95 billion, and China’s total gold reserves only ascend to 114,01 billion (0,114 trillion) (Figure 2).
A long twentieth century of imperialism over gold mining countries holds a current U.S. high advantage in monetary gold holdings, which implies a non-actually completed transition to China as a new hegemonic state of global capitalism. mining and mining struggles against U.S. gold mining and China copper mining in Peru evidenced a complex dynamic of imperialist mining from which approaches of U.S. hegemonic crisis and the initiation of a fifth systemic cycle of accumulation with “Chinese characteristics” can be problematized beyond.

In the current critical juncture of capitalism's systemic chaos (Arrighi and Silver 1999), antimining struggles in Los Andes and Latin America take an anti-imperialistic and antisystemic role. To initiate a fifth systemic cycle of accumulation, the top-layer of capitalists syndicated in the international bank capitalist state trusts requires investing the enormous magnitude of money-capital already accumulated in the branches of industry where larger profits can be obtained. Until new industries or future technological innovations could offer higher super-profit, their productive capital will be allocated intensively in the branches of industry they already know can provide the highest rate of profit, as mining and gold mining in particular. A new material expansion phase is a necessary condition for another cycle of financial expansion (Arrighi, 1994), but at this time the extended accumulation circuit mechanism could be reaching its natural, cultural and historical limits. Not only climate change but also historical change constrains and obstructs capitalism reconstitution at the present.

Until the beginning of the Twenty First century, the rising tide of environmental struggles carried out by peasant mobilization against the imperialists mining bourgeoisies, takes particular relevance to the development of class struggle historical process, as the militarized dimension of capital accumulation is exhibited by the militarist policy executed against the indigenous-cholo antimining struggles in Los Andes. Antimining struggles require dedicated study and analysis of their effects on the political economy of imperialism, and as a priority field of contention for class struggle development in the Andean region considering the centrality of the mining circuit of capital reproduction during a period of shared imperialism breakout, ecological and productive systemic chaos. Indeed, antimining struggles develop as limits to capital accumulation and imperialist domination in Los Andes. As O'Connor (1984) tried to remind to other radical thinkers in the middle of the 80's, Marxist theorists “left few clues regarding possible 'external barriers' to capitalist accumulation in the form of either 'limits to growth' determined by the availability of natural resources or [...] limits to accumulation determined by cultural-ideological conditions of economic and social reproduction” (O´Connor 1984:6).

Antisystemic environmental socialist struggles, as he would also state, are a central field of contention "to combat the attempt by capital and the state to ideologically construct and politically use the current crisis to restructure economic, political, and social life with the sole purpose of renewed capitalist accumulation, including a new 'long wave' of ideological innovation” (O'Connor 1984:23). Nevertheless, the material challenge that environmental socialist struggles display over militarized accumulation, as in antimining struggles in Los Andes, is showing a much further development not only restricted to the ideological struggles mentioned by James O'Connor. But bravely contesting effectively in absolute asymmetry of arms, the most powerful economic formations of the world-economy of imperialism executing its militarism. And in that manner constituting the "collective self" identity of the indigenous-cholo historical antisystemic resistance, in absolute antagonism with the reproduction of the international bank capitalist state trusts as the bearers of the finance capital circulating in the production and circulation of mining-commodity-capital, and most precious gold-money-commodity-capital.
